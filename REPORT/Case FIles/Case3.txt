Case Title
Navtej Singh Johar & Ors. vs. Union of India
Case Summary
Renowned dancer Navtej Singh Johar and other prominent members of the LGBTQ+ community filed a writ petition challenging the constitutional validity of Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code. This colonial-era law criminalized "carnal intercourse against the order of nature," effectively criminalizing consensual homosexual acts. The petitioners argued that this law violated their fundamental rights to privacy, dignity, equality, and freedom of expression, and subjected the LGBTQ+ community to discrimination and fear.
Case Details
Incident Description:
This petition was a significant step in the long legal struggle for LGBTQ+ rights in India. It was filed against a backdrop of widespread harassment and discrimination faced by the community under Section 377. The petitioners, all accomplished individuals, shared personal testimonies of the impact of this law on their lives. The petition sought to have the Supreme Court reconsider its 2013 decision in Suresh Kumar Koushal vs. Naz Foundation, which had recriminalized consensual homosexual acts.
Timeline of Events:
2001: The Naz Foundation files a petition in the Delhi High Court challenging Section 377.
July 2, 2009: The Delhi High Court decriminalizes consensual homosexual acts between adults.
December 11, 2013: The Supreme Court, in the Suresh Kumar Koushal case, overturns the Delhi High Court's decision.
April 2016: Navtej Singh Johar and others file a fresh writ petition in the Supreme Court.
January 8, 2018: The Supreme Court refers the petition to a larger Constitution Bench.
July 10, 2018: The final hearing begins before a five-judge Constitution Bench.
September 6, 2018: The Supreme Court delivers its landmark verdict.
Evidence:
The Writ Petition: A comprehensive petition with legal arguments and personal accounts from the petitioners.
Historical and Social Research: Reports on the origins of Section 377 and its adverse effects on the LGBTQ+ community.
Affidavits from Intervenors: Supporting statements from medical and psychological associations and other civil society groups.
Government’s Stand: The central government stated it would not contest the petition concerning consensual homosexual acts between adults in private, leaving the decision to the Court's "wisdom."
International Legal Precedents: Judgments from other countries that had decriminalized homosexuality.
Charges:
Section 377, IPC (Unnatural offences): This section penalized voluntary carnal intercourse "against the order of nature."
Constitutional Challenge: The petition contended that Section 377 violated Articles 14 (Right to Equality), 15 (Prohibition of Discrimination), 19(1)(a) (Freedom of Speech and Expression), and 21 (Right to Life and Personal Liberty).
