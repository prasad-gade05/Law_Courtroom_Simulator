Case Title
Joseph Shine vs. Union of India
Case Summary
Joseph Shine, a hotelier, filed a PIL challenging the constitutional validity of Section 497 of the Indian Penal Code, which criminalized adultery. The petitioner argued that the 158-year-old law was discriminatory as it only punished men for extramarital relationships, treating women as the property of their husbands, thereby violating the principles of gender equality and fundamental rights.
Case Details
Incident Description:
The petition was a public interest challenge to the law of adultery itself, rather than being based on a specific case involving the petitioner. Joseph Shine contended that Section 497 was a reflection of outdated Victorian morality and was manifestly arbitrary.[6][8] The law allowed a husband to prosecute the man who had an affair with his wife, but a wife had no such right. Furthermore, the law did not consider it an offense if the husband consented to the act. The petition sought to decriminalize adultery, arguing it should be a civil wrong and a ground for divorce, not a criminal offense.
Timeline of Events:
December 2017: Joseph Shine files the writ petition in the Supreme Court.
January 5, 2018: The Supreme Court issues a notice to the central government, agreeing to examine the law's validity.
July 11, 2018: The central government files an affidavit defending Section 497 to preserve the "sanctity of marriage."
August 1, 2018: A five-judge Constitution Bench commences hearings on the matter.
August 8, 2018: The Supreme Court reserves its judgment after hearing arguments.
September 27, 2018: The final verdict is delivered.
Evidence:
The Writ Petition: The petition outlining the constitutional challenge to Section 497.
Legal Precedents: Earlier judgments that had upheld the law, with arguments for why they should be reconsidered.
Historical Analysis: Examination of the colonial origins of the law.
Government's Affidavit: The Union of India's written defense of the provision.
Comparative Jurisprudence: Examples of the legal status of adultery in other countries.
Charges:
Section 497, IPC (Adultery): This section punished a man who had sexual intercourse with the wife of another man, without the husband's consent. The woman was exempt from punishment as an abettor.
Constitutional Challenge: The petition argued that Section 497 was violative of Articles 14 (Right to Equality), 15 (Prohibition of Discrimination), and 21 (Right to Life and Personal Liberty) of the Constitution.
