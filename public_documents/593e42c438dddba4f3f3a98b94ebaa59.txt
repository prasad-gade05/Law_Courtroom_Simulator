Proof, in that regard, can be let in by\n\nleading evidence by the accused. This is a question of\n\nfact    which    the    High     Court       may    not   go    into    in   the\n\npetition under <a href=\"/doc/1679850/\" id=\"a_9\">Section 482</a> of the Cr.PC.. The Court\n\nlater notices the amended <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_10\">Section 79</a> of the Act which\n\ncame into being with effect from 27.10.2009. It was\n\nnoted that it is only under the said amendment that a\n\nnon     obstante       clause     was        incorporated       keeping      the\n\napplication of other laws outside the purview in a fact\n\nsituation       covered     by    the    said       provision.      Under    the\n\namended    <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_11\">Section 79</a>,           in case       the intermediary          enters\n\ninto any conspiracy, the exemption under <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_12\">Section 79</a> of\n\nthe Act could not be claimed. So also, the intermediary\n\ncould     not    claim      exemption          in    case      he   fails     to\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_15\">                                        7</span>\n\fexpeditiously       remove     or        disable     access     to     the\n\nobjectionable material or unlawful activity, even after\n\nreceiving actual knowledge thereof.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Facts\" id=\"p_17\">9.   The   appellant,    it    was       found,    did   not   move    its\n\nlittle     finger   to   block       the    material      or   to     stop\n\ndissemination       of   the     unlawful          and   objectionable\n\nmaterial    in spite     of the      complainant issuing            notice\n\nabout    the   dissemination        of    defamatory     material      and\n\nunlawful activity on the part of the first accused.\n\nTherefore, it was found that the appellant could not\n\nclaim any exemption under <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_13\">Section 79</a> of the Act, prior\n\nto its substitution, or <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_14\">Section 79</a> of the Act, after\n\nits substitution, which took effect from 27.10.2009.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Facts\" id=\"p_18\">10. At this juncture, it is apposite that we took a\n\ndeeper look at what the Government of India has to say\n\nabout    <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_71\">Section 79</a>.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_92\">50. <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_72\">Section 79</a> is a safe harbour provision. The 2008 amendment introduced <a href=\"/doc/1965344/\" id=\"a_73\">Chapter XII to the\n\nInformation Technology Act</a>. The amendment was in the\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_86\">                                40</span>\n\fbackground of the decision of the Delhi High Court in\n\nAvinash Bajaj v. State (NCT of Delhi)20.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_101\">52. Intermediaries stand on a different footing being\n\nonly facilitators of exchanges of information or sales.\n\nPrior to the amendment, the exemption provision under\n\n<a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_74\">Section         79</a>     did      not        exist   and,      therefore,       an\n\nintermediary would have been liable for any third-party\n\ninformation or data made available by him as seen in\n\nthe Bazeed (supra).              After the amendment, intermediary\n\nis not liable under any Act if it satisfied certain\n\nrequirements as detailed in <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_75\">Section 79</a>.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_102\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"110813550\" id=\"span_87\">53. After            referring        to     the   decision      in     <a href=\"/doc/110813550/\" id=\"a_76\">Shreya\n\nSinghal</a> (supra), the Government of India has understood\n\nthe position at law to be that <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_77\">Section 79</a> stands read\n\ndown       to   mean     that    an        intermediary      would    need    to\n\ntakedown        information           only      upon    receiving       actual\n\nknowledge that a court order has been passed to remove\n\nor   disable         certain     material       and    not   otherwise.      The\n\nfurther stand of the Government of India is thus there\n\nis     a    recognition         that       intermediaries      and     neutral\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_88\">20</span>\n       116/2005 DLT 427\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_89\">                                           41</span>\n\fplatforms      are    only   facilitating         information.</span>     <span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"110813550\" id=\"span_90\">It    is\n\nfurther    pointed     out   on   behalf     of    the   Government      of\n\nIndia that the interpretation placed by this Court in\n\n<a href=\"/doc/110813550/\" id=\"a_78\">Shreya Singhal</a> (supra) was not available to the High\n\nCourt when it passed the impugned order in this case.\n\nShreya Singhal makes it clear that an intermediary\u2019s\n\nliability will not arise unless it failed to take down\n\nmaterial    upon there       being actual         knowledge by     court\n\norder or government communication. This safeguard has\n\nbeen put in place to avoid chilling affect on free\n\nspeech. The intermediaries would, if a contrary view is\n\ntaken, stand elevated to the status of super censors\n\nand   denude    the    internet    of   it   unique      feature    of   a\n\ndemocratic medium for all to publish, access and read\n\nany and all kinds of information.</span>\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_103\">54. Owing to the special unique characteristic of the\n\ninternet, intermediaries are not in a position to know\n\nabout a content which is posted on its platforms by\n\nitself and, therefore, the strict liability principle\n\ncannot be made applicable to internet intermediaries.\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_91\">                                  42</span>\n\fIt is the specific stand of the Government of India\n\nthat even pre-amendment, an intermediary could not know\n\nthe contents of what is posted on its website and,\n\ntherefore, be held liable in the absence of a takedown\n\norder by a court or Governmental Agency.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_104\">55. The Government of India, it is also noticed, has\n\nperceived a distinction between blocking under <a href=\"/doc/10190353/\" id=\"a_79\">Section\n\n69A</a>   of   the   Information   Technology   Act   and   takedown\n\nunder   <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_80\">Section 79</a>    of the    Information Technology     Act.\n\n<a href=\"/doc/10190353/\" id=\"a_81\">Section 69A</a> reads as In    fact,    the    learned      Senior   Counsel    for    the\n\nappellant did not, as such, canvass the matter on the\n\nbasis of <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_85\">Section 79</a> before it was substituted. On the question as to whether <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_87\">Section 79</a>, as it\n\nstood prior to the substitution, would provide a shield\n\nto     an   intermediary,          we    would     enter       the   following\n\nfindings.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_114\">60. In our view, <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_88\">Section 79</a>, before its substitution,\n\nexempted the Network Service Provider, which is defined\n\nas an intermediary, from liability under the Act, Rules\n\nor Regulations made thereunder in regard to any third-\n\nparty       information       or    data        made    available     by    him\n\nprovided the Service Provider:\n</p>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_26\">     1. The Service Provider proves that he had exercised\n\n       all due diligence to prevent the commissioning of\n\n       such offences or contraventions.\n</blockquote>\n\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_115\">61. This provision may be contrasted with the later\n\navtar       of   <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_89\">Section      79</a>    of     the     Act     consequent      upon\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_95\">                                        46</span>\n\fsubstitution with effect from 27.10.2009. Sub-Section\n\n(1) of <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_90\">Section 79</a>, in unambiguous words, declares by\n\nway of a non-obstante clause that in spite of anything\n\ncontained in any law which is in force, though subject\n\nto   the   provisions     of    sub-Sections   (2)     and    (3),   an\n\nintermediary would not be liable for any third-party\n\ninformation, data or communication link hosted by him.\n\nThe conditions are set out in sub-Section (2).\n\n\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_116\">62. As we have noticed, the scope of <a href=\"/doc/844026/\" id=\"a_91\">Section 79</a>, before\n\nits substitution, was confined to confer immunity from\n\nliability in regard to an offence under the Act or the\n\nRules     or Regulations       qua third-party     action or     data\n\nmade available. In Tiruvengadda    Mudali     v.\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_93\">           Tripurasundari     Ammal     [ILR 49 Mad\n           728] a Full Bench of the Madras High\n           Court observed that the exceptions to\n           <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_228\">Section 499</a> IPC, must be regarded as\n           exhaustive as to the cases which they\n           purport to cover and recourse can be had\n           to the English common law to add new\n           grounds of exception to those contained\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_169\">                               92</span>\n\f           in the statute. A person making libellous\n           statements in his complaint filed in\n           Court is not absolutely protected in a\n           criminal proceeding for defamation, for\n           under the Eighth Exception and the\n           illustration    to    Section   499    the\n           statements are privileged only when they\n           are   made   in  good   faith.  There   is\n           therefore authority for the proposition\n           that in determining the criminality of an\n           act under the Penal Code, 1860 the Courts\n           will not extend the scope of special\n           exceptions by resorting to the rule\n           peculiar to English common law that the\n           husband and wife are regarded as one.\u201d\n\n\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_181\">106. No    doubt,   the    Court      did   not   express   a     final\n\nopinion.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_182\">107. <span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"75389836\" id=\"span_170\">In    the   light    of   this     discussion,   we    may    only\n\nreiterate that the criminal offence of defamation under\n\n<a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_229\">Section 499</a> of the IPC is committed when a person makes\n\na defamatory imputation which, as explained in <a href=\"/doc/75389836/\" id=\"a_230\">Mohd.\n\nAbdulla Khan</a> (supra), would consist of the imputation\n\nbeing conveyed to the person about whom the imputation\n\nis made. In such\n\ncircumstances, what we are asked to do is to import in\n\nthe principles into the factual matrix when <a href=\"/doc/814605/\" id=\"a_295\">Section 79</a>\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_231\">                                  <a href=\"/doc/164072874/\" id=\"a_296\">143</a></span>\n\fwas differently worded and in proceedings under <a href=\"/doc/1679850/\" id=\"a_297\">Section\n\n482</a> of the Cr.PC. It is, undoubtedly, true that <a href=\"/doc/1378441/\" id=\"a_298\">Article\n\n19(1)(a)</a> and <a href=\"/doc/493243/\" id=\"a_299\">Article 19(2)</a> of the Constitution of India\n\nwere   very   much    available    in   2008   and   2009       though\n\n<a href=\"/doc/814605/\" id=\"a_300\">Section 79</a> was in its erstwhile avtar. In other words,\n\nwill it not be open to the appellant, assuming it to be\n\nthe intermediary, to contend that it cannot be called\n\nupon   to   remove,   defamatory    matter     comprised    in    any\n\nthird-party    information   without     there    being     a    court\n\norder?</span>\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_233\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"110813550\" id=\"span_232\">152. It is here that we would remind ourselves that we\n\nare called upon in this case to decide the correctness\n\nand legality of the order of the High Court passed in\n\nthe proceedings under <a href=\"/doc/1679850/\" id=\"a_301\">Section 482</a> of the Cr.PC. This\n\ncontention, as such, has not been raised. We notice, in\n\nfact, that in the very first ground, however, before\n\nthe High Court, it is contended that the appellant has\n\nno role. It has no control over the services provided\n\non the website. Thereafter, it is contended that even\n\nthe employees of the Parent Company do not have the\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_233\">                              144</span>\n\fability       to    remove         the    content          posted     on      the    blog\n\nwithout        an        order          from     a     court        of        competent\n\njurisdiction. It is the further case before the High\n\nCourt that under the law of United States of America\n\nwhich governed the functioning of the Parent Company,\n\nit is not obliged or required to remove any allegedly\n\ndefamatory content without the court order. There was\n\nno contention taken that on the basis that even if\n\nappellant          is    assumed         to     be    the     intermediary,           the\n\ncontinuance         of       the    articles         after    a   request       by   the\n\ncomplainant             to    remove          it,     would       not      constitute\n\npublication         at       the    hands      of     the    appellant         for   the\n\nreason that it will not constitute publication as there\n\nis no court order in India. It was also, no doubt, true\n\nthat    the    decision            of    this       Court    in     <a href=\"/doc/110813550/\" id=\"a_302\">Shreya      Singhal</a>\n\n(supra)       was       rendered         nearly       five     years       after      the\n\nimpugned       judgment            of    the        High     Court.</span>      <span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"110813550\" id=\"span_234\">As    already\n\nnoticed, what was <a href=\"/doc/110813550/\" id=\"a_303\">laid down in</a> <a href=\"/doc/110813550/\" id=\"a_304\">Shreya Singhal</a> (supra)\n\nwas    premised upon               the challenge            to <a href=\"/doc/183491832/\" id=\"a_305\">Section        79(3)(b)</a>\n\nwhich replaced the erstwhile avtar of <a href=\"/doc/814605/\" id=\"a_306\">Section 79</a> and\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_235\">                                           <a href=\"/doc/488246/\" id=\"a_307\">145</a></span>\n\falso a challenge to the Intermediary Rules of 2011,\n\nboth of which provisions came to be read down by the\n\ncourt.</span>\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_234\">153. What appellant is asking this Court to do is to\n\ndecide   the      point     which    is    not   even      raised     as     such\n\nbefore the High Court. Having noticed the exact ground\n\ntaken    before       the    High     Court,         it   relates      to     the\n\ninability    of       the   Parent    Company        to   remove      the    post\n\nwithout the court order. However, we feel that this is\n\na   question      which     can     be,    independent         of   the      non-\n\navailability of the protection under <a href=\"/doc/814605/\" id=\"a_308\">Section 79</a> of the\n\nAct in its erstwhile avtar, pursued by the appellant.\n\nThis    is   a    matter     which     we      leave      it   open    to     the\n\nappellant to urge before the Court.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_235\">154. There       is   a   complaint       by   the    appellant       that    the\n\nMagistrate did not have territorial jurisdiction over\n\nthe appellant and the procedure under <a href=\"/doc/443138/\" id=\"a_309\">Section 203</a> of\n\nthe Cr.PC should have been followed. We noticed that\n\nthis contention was not raised before the High Court.\n\nWe further noticed that on this ground alone, in the\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_236\">                                     146</span>\n\ffacts of this case, after nearly a decade of the matter\n\npending in this Court, it would be unjust to remand the\n\nmatter back to the Magistrate, which we must indeed\n\nnote, was one of the submissions of the appellant.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_236\">155. The upshot of the above discussion is as follows:\n\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_167\">1. We reject the contention of the appellant that the\n\n  High   Court   should    have   acted   on    the     Google   LLC\n\n  conditions and found that the appellant is not the\n\n  intermediary.   We     hold   that   this    is   a   matter   for\n\n  trial.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_168\">2. We hold that <a href=\"/doc/814605/\" id=\"a_310\">Section 79</a> of the Act, prior to its\n\n  substitution,    did    not   protect   an    intermediary      in\n\n  regard to the offence under <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_311\">Section 499</a>/<a href=\"/doc/1408202/\" id=\"a_312\">500</a> of the\n\n  IPC.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_169\">3. We set aside the findings by the High Court regarding\n\n  the alleged refusal of the appellant to respond to\n\n  the notice to remove. We make it clear, however, that\n\n  it is for the Court to decide the matter on the basis\n\n  of the materials placed before it, and taking into\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_237\">                                147</span>\n\f  consideration,     the   observations     contained    in    this\n\n  judgment.\n</blockquote>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_237\">156. We leave open the contentions of the parties except\n\nthose which we have finally pronounced upon. Subject to\n\nthe   above,   the   Magistrate     shall   proceed     with    the\n\ncomplaint.
