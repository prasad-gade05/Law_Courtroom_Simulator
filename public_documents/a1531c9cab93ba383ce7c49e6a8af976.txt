Appellant\n\n                                Versus\n\nState of Maharashtra &amp; Ors.                  ...Respondents\n\n\n\n                               J U D G M E N T\n</pre>\n\n\n<p data-structure=\"Issue\" id=\"p_1\">Dipak Misra, J.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Issue\" id=\"p_2\">The Controversy\nThe seminal issue that spiralled in the course of  hearing  of  this  appeal\ncentres around the question framed vide  order  dated  18.2.2015,  for  this\nCourt thought it apposite to answer, whether the poem  titled  \u201cGandhi  Mala\nBhetala\u201d (\u2018I met Gandhi\u2019) in the magazine named  the  \u2018Bulletin\u2019  which  was\npublished,  in  July-August,  1994  issue,  meant  for  private  circulation\namongst the members of All  India  Bank  Association  Union,  could  in  the\nultimate eventuate give rise to framing of  charge  under  <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_1\">Section  292</a>  IPC\nagainst the author, the publisher and  the  printer.   The  question  framed\nreads thus:-\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_1\">\u201cRegard being had to the  importance  of  the  matter,  we  had  sought  the\nassistance of Mr. Fali S. Nariman, learned senior  counsel,  to  assist  the\nCourt, and he has gladly rendered.  At the time of hearing,  we  have  asked\nthe learned senior counsel, learned Amicus Curiae, to assist  the  Court  as\nregards the proposition whether in a write-up or a  poem,  keeping  in  view\nthe concept and conception of poetic license and the liberty  of  perception\nand expression, use the name of a historically respected personality by  way\nof allusion or symbol is permissible.\u201d\n\n      Mr. Gopal Subramanium,  learned  senior  counsel,  appearing  for  the\nappellant, in his written note  of  submissions,  has  segregated  the  said\nquestion into five parts, namely, (a) whether there could be a reference  to\na historically respected personality; (b) could that reference be by way  of\nallusion or symbol; (c) could that allusion be resorted to in a write-up  or\na poem; (d) whether the conception and concept  of  poetic  license  permits\nadopting an allusion; and  (e)  whether  any  of  the  above  could  involve\nascribing words or acts to a historically reputed  personality  which  could\nappear obscene to a reader.  He has urged with solemn  vehemence  that  when\nthe  author  is  not  represented  before  the  Court,  adjudication  on  an\nimportant issue which  fundamentally  relates  to  freedom  of  thought  and\nexpression, would be inappropriate and a poem or  a  write-up  is  indeed  a\npart of free speech and expression, as perceived under <a href=\"/doc/1378441/\" id=\"a_2\">Article  19(1)(a)</a>  of\nthe Constitution and that apart the expression \u201cpoetic licence\u201d  is  neither\na concept nor a conception because  the  idea  of  a  poetic  freedom  is  a\nguaranteed and an enforceable fundamental right and this  Court  should  not\ndetract and convert it into a  permissive  licence.   Additionally,  learned\nsenior counsel has contended that quintessential liberty of  perception  and\nexpression  when  placed  in  juxtaposition  with   \u201cpoetic   licence\u201d,   is\ninapposite since the expression \u201cpermissible\u201d sounds a discordant note  with\n\u201cliberty of perception and  expression\u201d,  a  sacrosanct  fundamental  right,\nintegral to human dignity, thought, feeling, behaviour, expression  and  all\njural concepts of human freedom guaranteed not only under  the  Constitution\nbut even recognised under the International Covenants, for  they  can  never\nbe placed in the company or association of expressions such as \u201clicense\u201d  or\n\u201cpermissibility\u201d.    Emphasising  on   the   said   facet,   submitted   Mr.\nSubramanium that the Constitution has liberated the citizens from  \u2018license\u2019\nand \u2018permissibility\u2019,  which  are  expressions  of  disempowerment  and  the\nentire freedom struggle was centered  around  the  concept  of  empowerment.\nThere is a suggestion in the  written  note  of  submissions  to  place  the\nmatter before a Bench of five Judges as enshrined under  <a href=\"/doc/244297/\" id=\"a_3\">Article  145(3)</a>  of\nthe Constitution.  In spite of the said submission, learned senior  counsel,\nwe must appreciably state, has copiously dealt with  the  issues  that  have\nemerged from the question, in his written note of submissions.\nMr. Fali S. Nariman, learned senior counsel and amicus curiae supported  the\nphraseology in the question  with  immense  intellectual  vigour,  patience,\nperseverance and endeavour and submitted that the issue that this Court  has\nthought of addressing is absolutely  invulnerable  and  unalterable  as  the\nConstitution of India does not recognise absolute freedom and <a href=\"/doc/493243/\" id=\"a_4\">Article  19(2)</a>\nof the  Constitution  regulates  the  same  and  <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_5\">Section  292</a>  IPC  being  a\nprovision which is saved by <a href=\"/doc/493243/\" id=\"a_6\">Article 19(2)</a>, the presence or  absence  of  the\nauthor is immaterial; what is to be seen is whether  the  poem  prima  facie\nexhibits obscenity, especially,  in  the  context  of  Mahatma  Gandhi,  the\n\u201cFather of the Nation\u201d,  as  the  identity  of  the  historically  respected\npersonality is absolutely clear and there is no scintilla of  doubt  in  the\nmind of any average reader.  Learned amicus curiae  would  submit  that  the\nquestion deserves to be dealt with and answered in proper perspective.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_2\">Clarification of the question framed\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_3\">2.    We are obligated to clarify the position.  It is  apt  to  state  here\nthe question framed by us has to be contextually understood.   The  question\nwas framed in the factual matrix of the case.  The proposition presented  is\nthat  despite  all  the  poetic  licence  and  liberty  of  perception   and\nexpression, whether \u2018poem\u2019 or \u2018write-up\u2019 can use the name of a  historically\nrespected personality by way of an allusion or symbol in an obscene  manner.\n \u201cHistorically respected personality\u201d was used in the backdrop  of  the  use\nof  the  name  of  Mahatma  Gandhi.  When  the  name  of  such  a  respected\npersonality is used as an allusion or symbol, and  language  is  revoltingly\nsuggestive whether that is likely to come within  the  perceived  ambit  and\nsweep of <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_7\">Section 292</a> IPC, whether it is permissible.  We  shall  dwell  upon\nthis facet when we will discuss the poem in a prima facie  manner,  for  the\npurpose of scrutinising the order framing charge; and  we  shall  also  deal\nwith the submission of Mr.  Subramanium,  which  has  been  assiduously  put\nforth by him that the name of Gandhi has been used as a  surrealistic  voice\nand hence, the poet  is  entitled  to  use  the  language  as  a  medium  of\nexpression  in  the  poem.   We  do  not  intend  to  catalogue   names   of\nhistorically respected personalities as that is not an issue in  this  case.\nHere the case rests on the poem titled \u201cI met Gandhi\u201d.  As far as the  words\n\u201cpoetic license\u201d, are concerned, it can never remotely  mean  a  license  as\nused or understood in the language of law.     There  is  no  authority  who\ngives a license to a poet.  These are words from the  realm  of  literature.\nThe poet assumes his own freedom which is allowed to him by the  fundamental\nconcept of poetry.  He is free to depart from the  reality;  fly  away  from\ngrammar; walk in glory by not following the systematic  metres;  coin  words\nat his  own  will;  use  archaic  words  to  convey  thoughts  or  attribute\nmeanings; hide ideas beyond  myths  which  can  be  absolutely  unrealistic;\ntotally pave a path where neither rhyme nor rhythm prevail; can put  serious\nideas in satires, ifferisms, notorious repartees;  take  aid  of  analogies,\nmetaphors, similes in his own style,  compare  like  \u201clife  with  sandwiches\nthat is consumed everyday\u201d or  \u201clife  is  like  peeling  of  an  onion\u201d,  or\n\u201csociety is like a stew\u201d; define ideas that can balloon into the  sky  never\nto come down; cause violence to logic  at  his  own  fancy;  escape  to  the\nsphere  of  figurative  truism;  get  engrossed  in   \u201cuniversal   eye   for\nresemblance\u201d, and one can do nothing except writing a critical  appreciation\nin his own manner and according to  his understanding.  When the  poet  says\n\u201cI saw eternity yesterday  night\u201d,  no  reader  would  understand  the  term\n\u2018eternity\u2019 in its prosaic sense.  The Hamletian question has many  a  layer;\neach  is  free  to  confer  a  meaning;  be  it  traditional  or  modern  or\nindividualistic.  No one can stop a dramatist or  a  poet  or  a  writer  to\nwrite freely expressing  his  thoughts  and  similarly  none  can  stop  the\ncritics to give their comments whatever its worth.  One may  concentrate  on\nclassical facets and one may think at a metaphysical  level  or  concentrate\non romanticism as is understood in the poems of Keats, Byron or  Shelley  or\none may dwell on the nature and write poems like  William  Wordsworth  whose\npoems, say some, are as didactic.  One may  also  venture  to  compose  like\nAlexander Pope or Dryden or get into individual modernism like  Ezra  Pound,\nT.S. Eliot or Pablo Neruda.   That is fundamentally what is meant by  poetic\nlicense.\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_4\">3.    We may slightly delve into the area in Sanskrit literature  that  gave\nimmense emphasis on aesthetics.  The concept of  rasa  though  mentioned  in\nthe Vedas and by Valmiki gets consummate expression in  all  its  complexity\nwith  Bharata  when  he  introduces  it  to  explain  aesthetic  experience.\n\u201cVibhavanubhav  vyabhichari  sanyogadrasnishpati\u201d.   Bharata  discusses   in\ndetail the contributing factors like vibhavas,  anubhavas,  vybhicharibhavas\nand sthayibhavas.  Dandin emphasises on lucidity,  sweetness,  richness  and\ngrandeur to  basically  constitute  poetry  and  that  is  why  it  is  said\n\u201cDandinha Padlalityam\u201d.   Some critics like Vamana,  stressing  on  soul  of\npoetry perceive \u2018riti\u2019 as \u201cRitiraatma kavyasya\u201d.   Some  also  subscribe  to\nthe theory that \u2018rasa\u2019 gets expressed through dhvani.   There  are  thinkers\nwho compare writings of T.S. Eliot, when he  states  poetic  delineation  of\nsentiments and feelings, to have the potentiality of being  associated  with\nthe \u2018element of surprise\u2019 which is essential to  poetry,  and  there  he  is\nakin to Indian poeticians like Kuntaka who called  poetry  \u2018vakrokti\u2019  which\nhe explains as \u201cvaidagdhyabhangibhaniti\u201d \u2013 a mode  of  expression  depending\non the peculiar turn given to it by the skill of the poet.   Some  emphasise\non \u201cbest words used in best  order\u201d  so  that  poem  can  attain  style  and\nelevation.  To put it differently, the \u2018poetic licence\u2019 can have  individual\nfeatures, deviate from norm, may form collective characteristics or  it  may\nhave a linguistic freedom wider than a syntax sentence compass.\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_5\">4.    We have emphasised on these facets as we are disposed  to  think  that\nthe manner in which the learned senior counsel has suggested the meaning  of\n\u2018poetic license\u2019 is not apt.  Freedom of writing is not in  question.   That\ncannot be.  And we say so without any fear of contradiction.\n</p><p data-structure=\"Facts\" id=\"p_6\">5.    In course of our judgment, we shall deal with the  other  facets  that\nhave been so assiduously put forth by Mr. Subramanium and  so  indefatigably\ncontroverted by Mr. Fali S. Nariman, learned amicus curiae.\nThe factual score\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_7\">6.    As far as the suggestion given for placing the matter before  a  five-\nJudge Bench, we are of the considered view that there is  no  need  for  the\nsame.\n</p><p data-structure=\"Facts\" id=\"p_8\">7.    Presently, we shall state the exposition of facts.  On the basis of  a\ncomplaint lodged by one V.V. Anaskar, a resident of Pune, and  a  member  of\n\u2018Patit Pawan Sangthan\u2019, with the Commissioner of  Police,  relating  to  the\npublication of the poem, which was published, in  July-August,  1994,  meant\nfor private circulation amongst the members of All  India  Bank  Association\nUnion, a crime was registered as FIR No. 7/95 at P.S. Gandhi  Chowk,  Latur,\non being transferred from Pune, for the offences punishable  under  <a href=\"/doc/345634/\" id=\"a_8\">Sections\n153-A</a> and  <a href=\"/doc/771276/\" id=\"a_9\">153-B</a>  read  with  <a href=\"/doc/37788/\" id=\"a_10\">Section  34</a>,  IPC  and  eventually  after  due\ninvestigation charge sheet was filed for the said offences along  with  292,\n<a href=\"/doc/1569253/\" id=\"a_11\">IPC</a>  against  the  present  appellant,  the  publisher  and   the   printer,\nrespondent no.3, of the Bulletin  and  the  author,  one  Vasant  Dattatraya\nGujar.  When the matter was pending before the  Chief  Judicial  Magistrate,\nLatur, all the accused persons filed an application for  discharge  and  the\nlearned Magistrate by order  dated  4.5.2001  held  that  no  case  for  the\noffences under <a href=\"/doc/345634/\" id=\"a_12\">Sections  153-A</a>  and  <a href=\"/doc/771276/\" id=\"a_13\">153-B</a>  was  made  out  and  accordingly\ndischarged them of the said offences but declined to do  so  in  respect  of\nthe offence under <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_14\">Section 292</a>, IPC.  On a revision being filed, the  learned\nAdditional Sessions Judge did not think it  appropriate  to  interfere  with\nthe order passed by the  trial  Magistrate  which  constrained  the  accused\npersons to invoke jurisdiction under <a href=\"/doc/1679850/\" id=\"a_15\">Section 482</a> of the CrPC  and  the  High\nCourt of Bombay, Aurangabad  Bench  dismissed  the  application.   The  said\ndecision is the subject of matter of this appeal by  special  leave  at  the\ninstance of the publisher.  The author has chosen not to  assail  the  order\npassed by the High Court.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Issue\" id=\"p_9\">Concept of obscenity\n</p><p data-structure=\"PetArg\" id=\"p_10\">8.    Apart from submitting that the orders passed by  all  the  Courts  are\nabsolutely perverse and deserve to be lancinated, it  is  submitted  by  Mr.\nSubramanium, learned senior counsel that to appreciate the  question  framed\nby this Court, despite his reservation on the legal  score  as  regards  its\nphraseology, the meaning of  the  term  \u201cobscenity\u201d  has  to  be  appositely\nunderstood.  He has referred to the  Black\u2019s  Law  Dictionary  that  defines\nobscenity as follows:-\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_3\">\u201cObscene, adj. (16c) -  Extremely  offensive  under  contemporary  community\nstandards of morality  and  decency;  grossly  repugnant  to  the  generally\naccepted notions of what is appropriate. Under the  Supreme  Court&#x27;s  three-\npart test, material is legally obscene - and therefore not  protected  under\nthe First Amendment - if, taken as a whole, the material (1) appeals to  the\nprurient interest in sex, as  determined  by  the  average  person  applying\ncontemporary  community  standards;  (2)   portrays   sexual   conduct,   as\nspecifically defined by the applicable state law, in  a  patently  offensive\nway; and (3) lacks serious  literary,  artistic,  political,  or  scientific\nvalue. Miller v. Callifornia, 413 U.<a href=\"/doc/178237634/\" id=\"a_16\">S. 15</a>, <a href=\"/doc/1184400/\" id=\"a_17\">93</a> S.Ct. 2607 (1973).\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_4\">If there be no abstract definition, ... should not  the  word  &#x27;obscene&#x27;  be\nallowed to indicate the present critical point  in  the  compromise  between\ncandor and shame at which the community may  have  arrived  here  and  now?\u201d\nUnited States V Kennerley, 209 F. 119, 121 (S.D.N.Y.1913) (per Hand.J.)\u201d\n\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_11\">9.    The learned senior counsel has also referred to the  decision  of  the\nAllahabad          High          Court          in          Kamla\u00a0Kant\u00a0Singh\nVs.\u00a0Chairman/ManagingDirector,\u00a0Bennetta\u00a0Colman\u00a0and    Company    Ltd.    and\nOrs.[1], wherein  the  High  Court  dealt  with  the  meaning  of  the  word\n\u2018obscenity\u2019.  The delineation is as follows:-\n</p>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_5\">\u201c15. The word obscenity  has  been  explained  in  \u2018Jowitts&#x27;  Dictionary  of\nEnglish Law as follows:\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_6\">\u201cAn article is deemed to be obscene, if its effect,  or  where  the  article\ncomprises two or more distinct items, the effect of any one of its items  if\ntaken as a whole, is to tend to deprave and corrupt persons, who are  likely\nhaving regard to all the relevant circumstances to  read,  to  see  or  hear\nmatters contained or embodied in it. (See R. v. Claytone and  Hasley,\u00a0(1963)\n1 QB 163, R. v. Anderson,\u00a0(1972) 1 QB 304)&quot;.  Obscenity  and  depravity  are\nnot confined to sex. (See John Calder Publications v.  Powell,\u00a0(1965)  1  QB\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_7\">509.)\n\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_8\">16. \u2026According to  Black&#x27;s  Law  Dictionary  obscenity  means  character  or\nquality of being obscene, conduct, tending to corrupt the public  merely  by\nits  indecency  or  lewdness.  According  to  Webster&#x27;s  New   International\nDictionary, word &#x27;obscene&#x27; means disgusting to the senses,  usually  because\nof some filthy grotesque or unnatural  quality,  grossly  repugnant  to  the\ngenerally accepted notions of what is appropriate.\u201d\n\n\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_12\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"445006\" id=\"span_1\">10.   The High Court of Madras in <a href=\"/doc/445006/\" id=\"a_18\">Public Prosecutor  v.  A.D.  Sabapathy</a>[2],\nhas opined that the word \u201cobscene\u201d must be given its  ordinary  and  literal\nmeaning,  that  is,  \u2018repulsive\u2019,  \u2018filthy\u2019,  \u2018loathsome\u2019,  \u2018indecent\u2019   and\n\u2018lewd\u2019.</span>  The learned senior counsel has also referred  to  the  judgment  of\nSupreme Court of Canada in R. v.  Beaver[3],  wherein  Maclaren,  J.A.,  has\ndefined \u2018obscene\u2019 as follows:-\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_9\">\u201cThe word &#x27;obscene&#x27; \u2026 was originally used to describe  anything  disgusting,\nrepulsive, filthy or foul. The use of the word is now said  to  be  somewhat\narchaic or poetic; and it is ordinarily restricted  to  something  offensive\nto modesty or decency, or  expressing  or  suggesting  unchaste  or  lustful\nideas, or being impure, indecent, or lewd.&quot;\n</blockquote>\n\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_13\">11.   After generally referring  to  the  meaning  of  the  term  obscenity,\nlearned senior counsel has  emphasised  on  the  tests  adopted  in  various\ncountries relating to obscenity.  Mr. Subramanium has  referred  to  various\nauthorities of United Kingdom, United States  of  America,  European  Courts\nand this Court to pyramid the proposition that the tests laid down by  legal\nsystem including the authorities of this Court do not suggest that that  the\ninstant poem can remotely be treated as  obscene.   First,  we  shall  dwell\nupon the tests and standards laid by various Courts  and  then  the  binding\nauthorities of this Court and  thereafter  to  the  concept  of  freedom  of\nspeech and expression on the constitutional parameters and finally delve  to\nadjudge the facet of obscenity and address applicability of  the  determined\ntest in the context of the question and ultimately the nature  of  the  poem\nand the justifiability of the order impugned.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Issue\" id=\"p_14\">Test evolved in United Kingdom\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_15\">12.   As far as United Kingdom is concerned, Mr.  Subramanium  has  referred\nto Regina v. Hicklin[4], the meaning given by Cockburn C.J.  and  drawn  our\nattention to the Article by  J.E.  Hall  Williams  in  Obscenity  in  Modern\nEnglish Law[5] wherein the learned  author  observed  that  Hicklin  (supra)\ngave a complete go by to the principle  of  \u201cmens  rea\u201d  which  propounds  a\ncertain degree of  protection  to  the  accused.   The  learned  author  was\ncritical on the concept of presumption as  propounded  in  Hicklin  (supra).\nIn the said article, learned author referred to certain observations  in  R.\nv. Martin Secker &amp; Warburg LD[6].  In the said case, Stable J. has stated\n\u201cThe test of obscenity to be applied today is extracted from a  decision  of\n1868; it is this:  \u201c\u2026.  Whether  the  tendency  of  the  matter  charged  as\nobscenity is to deprave and corrupt  those  whose  mind  are  open  to  such\nimmoral influences, and into whose hands a  publication  of  this  sort  may\nfall.\u201d  Because this test was laid down in 1868, that  does  not  mean  that\nyou have to consider whether this book is an obscene book by  the  standards\nof nearly a century ago.  Your task is to decide whether you think that  the\ntendency of the book is to deprave those whose minds today are open to  such\nimmoral influences and into whose hands the book may fall in this  year,  or\nlast year when it was published in this country.   Considering  the  curious\nchange of approach from one age to  another,  it  is  not  uninteresting  to\nobserve that in the  course  of  the  argument  of  the  case  in  1868  the\nrhetorical question was asked: \u201cWhat can be more obscene than many  pictures\n\u201cpublicly exhibited, as the Venus in the Dulwich Gallery?\u201d  There  are  some\nwho think with reverence that man is fashioned in the image of God, and  you\nknow that babies are not born in this world, be they of either sex,  dressed\nup in a frock-coat or an equivalent feminine garment.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_16\">We are not sitting here as judges of taste.  We are not here to say  whether\nwe like a book of this kind.  We are not here to say  whether  we  think  it\nwould be a good thing if books like this were never written.  You  are  here\ntrying a criminal charge and in a criminal court you cannot find  a  verdict\nof \u201cGuilty\u201d against the accused  unless,  on  the  evidence  that  you  have\nheard, you and each one of you are fully satisfied that the  charge  against\nthe accused person has been proved.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_17\">Remember the charge is a charge that the tendency of the book is to  corrupt\nand deprave.  The charge is not that the tendency of the book is  either  to\nshock or to disgust.  That is not a criminal offence.  Then you say:  \u201cWell,\ncorrupt or \u201cdeprave whom?\u201d and again the test: those whose  minds  are  open\nto such immoral influences and into whose hands a publication of  this  sort\nmay fall.  What, exactly, does that mean?   Are  we  to  take  our  literary\nstandards as being the level of something that is suitable for  a  fourteen-\nyear-old school girl?  Or do we go even further back than that, and  are  we\nto be reduced to the sort of  books  that  one  reads  as  a  child  in  the\nnursery?  The answer to that is: Of  course  not.   A  mass  of  literature,\ngreat literature, from many angles is wholly unsuitable for reading  by  the\nadolescent, but that does not  mean  that  the  publisher  is  guilty  of  a\ncriminal offence for making those works available to the general public.\u201d\n\n      In the ultimate eventuate, the learned Judge concluded, thus:-\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_10\">\u201cI do not suppose there is a decent man or woman in this court who does  not\nwhole-heartedly believe that pornography, the  filthy  bawdy  muck  that  is\njust filth for filth\u2019s sake, ought to be stamped out and  suppressed. In Roth v. United States[10], the principal question was  whether  the\nFederal  Obscenity  Statute  violated  the  First  Amendment   of   the   US\nConstitution which guaranteed freedom of speech. The  section  of  the  Penal  Code  in  dispute  was\nintroduced by the Obscene Publications Act, 1925 (7 of 1925) to give  effect\nof the International  Convention  for  the  suppression  of  or  traffic  in\nobscene publications signed by India in 1923  at  Geneva.  It  does  not  go\nbeyond obscenity which falls directly within the words \u201cpublic  decency  and\nmorality\u201d  of  the  second  clause  of  the  article.  The  word,   as   the\ndictionaries tell us, denotes the  quality  of  being  obscene  which  means\noffensive to modesty or decency; lewd, filthy and repulsive.  It  cannot  be\ndenied that it is an important interest of society  to  suppress  obscenity.\nThere is, of course, some difference between obscenity  and  pornography  in\nthat the latter denotes writings, pictures etc. intended  to  arouse  sexual\ndesire while the former may include writings etc. not intended to do so  but\nwhich have that tendency. Both, of course,  offend  against  public  decency\nand morals but pornography is obscenity in a more aggravated form.  Mr  Garg\nseeks to limit action to cases of intentional lewdness  which  he  describes\nas dirt for dirt&#x27;s sake and which has now received the appellation of  hard-\ncore pornography by which term is meant libidinous writings of  high  erotic\neffect unredeemed by anything literary or artistic and  intended  to  arouse\nsexual feelings.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_68\">8. That cherished  right  on\nwhich our democracy rests is meant for the expression of  free  opinions  to\nchange political or social  conditions  or  for  the  advancement  of  human\nknowledge. While  dealing  with\nthe <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_122\">section 292</a> IPC, the court held thus:-\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_99\">\u201c24. Coming to the substance of the complaints,  we  fail  to  see  how  the\nappellant\u2019s remarks amount to \u201cobscenity\u201d in  the  context  of  <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_123\">Section  292</a>\nIPC. sub-section (1) of Section 292 states that the publication of  a  book,\npamphlet, paper, writing, drawing, painting,  representation,  figure,  etc.\nwill be deemed obscene, if\u2014\n\n\u2022  It is lascivious (i.e. expressing or causing sexual desire); or\n\n\u2022  Appeals to the prurient  interest  (i.e.  excessive  interest  in  sexual\nmatters); or\n\n\u2022  If its effect, or the effect of any one of the items,  tends  to  deprave\nand corrupt persons, who are  likely  to  read,  see,  or  hear  the  matter\ncontained in such materials.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_100\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"1623275\" id=\"span_30\">In the past, authors as well as publishers of artistic  and  literary  works\nhave been put to trial and punished under this section.\u201d\n\nThereafter, the court referred  to  the  authorities  in  <a href=\"/doc/1623275/\" id=\"a_124\">Ranjit  D.  Udeshi</a>\n(supra) and Samresh Bose (surpa) and proceeded to observe:-</span>\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_101\">\u201c45. Even though the constitutional freedom of speech and expression is  not\nabsolute and can be subjected to reasonable restrictions on grounds such  as\n\u201cdecency and morality\u201d among others, we must  lay  stress  on  the  need  to\ntolerate unpopular views in the sociocultural  space.  The  Framers  of  our\nConstitution recognised the importance of safeguarding this right since  the\nfree flow of opinions and ideas is essential to sustain the collective  life\nof the  citizenry.  While  an  informed  citizenry  is  a  precondition  for\nmeaningful governance in  the  political  sense,  we  must  also  promote  a\nculture of open dialogue when it comes to societal attitudes.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_102\">46. Admittedly, the appellant\u2019s remarks did provoke a controversy since  the\nacceptance of premarital sex and live-in relationships is viewed by some  as\nan attack on the centrality of marriage. While there can be  no  doubt  that\nin India, marriage is an important social institution,  we  must  also  keep\nour minds open to the fact that there are certain individuals or groups  who\ndo not hold the same view. To be sure,  there  are  some  indigenous  groups\nwithin our country wherein sexual relations outside the marital setting  are\naccepted as a normal occurrence. Even in the societal mainstream, there  are\na significant [pic]number of people who see nothing  wrong  in  engaging  in\npremarital sex. Notions of social morality  are  inherently  subjective  and\nthe criminal law cannot be used as a means  to  unduly  interfere  with  the\ndomain of personal autonomy. Morality and criminality are not coextensive.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_103\">47. In the present case, the substance of the controversy  does  not  really\ntouch on whether premarital sex is socially acceptable.  Instead,  the  real\nissue of  concern  is  the  disproportionate  response  to  the  appellant\u2019s\nremarks. If the  complainants  vehemently  disagreed  with  the  appellant\u2019s\nviews, then they should have contested her views through the news  media  or\nany other public platform. The law should not be used in a manner  that  has\nchilling effects on the \u201cfreedom of speech and expression\u201d.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_104\">            xxx        xxx        xxx\n\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_105\">50. Thus, dissemination  of  news  and  views  for  popular  consumption  is\npermissible  under  our  constitutional  scheme.  The  different  views  are\nallowed to be expressed by  the  proponents  and  opponents.  A  culture  of\nresponsible reading  is  to  be  inculcated  amongst  the  prudent  readers.\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_73\">Morality and criminality are far from being coextensive.  An  expression  of\nopinion in favour of non-dogmatic and non-conventional morality  has  to  be\ntolerated as the same cannot be a ground to penalise the author.\u201d\n\n      The aforesaid authority, thus, emphasises on the  need  for  tolerance\nof unpopular views in the socio-cultural space.  It also takes note  of  the\nfact  that  notions  of  social  morality  are  inherently  subjective;  and\nmorality and criminality are not co-extensive.  It is apt to note here  that\nin the said case, the Court has also held  that  by  the  statement  of  the\nappellant therein no offence was committed.  The Court recognised that  free\nflow of notions and ideas is essential to sustain the  collective  lives  of\nthe citizenry.\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_74\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"195958005\" id=\"span_31\">58.   Recently in <a href=\"/doc/195958005/\" id=\"a_125\">Aveek Sarkar and another  v.  State  of  West  Bengal  and\nothers</a>[62], the Court was  dealing  with  the  fact  situation  where  Boris\nBecker, a world renowned tennis  player,  had  posed  nude  with  his  dark-\nskinned fianc\u00e9e by name Barbara Feltus, a film actress.  Both of them  spoke\nfreely about their engagement, their lives and  future  plans.  The  article\nprojected Boris Becker as a strident protester of  the  pernicious  practice\nof \u201cApartheid\u201d and the purpose of the photograph was also  to  signify  that\nlove champions over  hatred.   The  article  was  published  in  the  German\nmagazine by name \u201cStern\u201d. \u201cSports World\u201d, a widely circulated  magazine  had\nreproduced the photograph and the  article  as  cover  story.   \u201cAnandabazar\nPatrika\u201d, a newspaper having wide circulation in Kolkata, also published  in\nthe second page of the newspaper the photograph as  it  appeared  in  Sports\nWorld.  A lawyer claiming to be a regular reader of Sports World as well  as\nAnandabazar Patrika filed a complaint under <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_126\">Section 292</a> of IPC  against  the\nappellants therein, the Editor, the Publisher and Printer of  the  newspaper\nand also against the Editor  of  Sports  World,  former  Captain  of  Indian\nCricket Team, Late Mansoor Ali Khan Pataudi.  The  learned  Magistrate  took\ncognizance and issued summons under <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_127\">Section 292</a>, IPC and also under  <a href=\"/doc/1098494/\" id=\"a_128\">Section\n4</a> of the 1986 Act.  The appellants approached the High  Court  for  quashing\nthe criminal  proceeding  but  the  High  Court  declined  to  exercise  the\njurisdiction under <a href=\"/doc/1679850/\" id=\"a_129\">Section 482</a> CrPC.  It was  contended  before  this  Court\nthat obscenity has to be  judged  in  the  context  of  contemporary  social\nmores, current socio-moral attitude  of  the  community  and  the  prevalent\nnorms of acceptability/susceptibility  of  the  community,  in  relation  to\nmatters in issue.</span>  <span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"25099\" id=\"span_32\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"1623275\" id=\"span_33\">Reliance was placed on the  Constitution  Bench  decision\nin <a href=\"/doc/1623275/\" id=\"a_130\">Ranjit D. Udeshi</a> (supra)  and  <a href=\"/doc/25099/\" id=\"a_131\">Chandrakant  Kalyandas  Kakodkar</a>  (supra).</span></span>\n<span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"1383068\" id=\"span_34\">The two-Judge Bench referred to the principles stated in the  aforesaid  two\ndecisions and the principles stated in <a href=\"/doc/1383068/\" id=\"a_132\">Samresh Bose</a> (supra).</span>  <span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"1383068\" id=\"span_35\">While  quoting\na passage from <a href=\"/doc/1383068/\" id=\"a_133\">Samresh Bose</a>  (supra),  the  Court  observed  that  the  view\nexpressed therein was the contemporary social standards in  the  year  1985.\nThe Court further observed that while judging a particular  photograph,  and\nthe article of the newspaper as obscene in 2014, regard must be had  to  the\ncontemporary mores and the national standards and not  the  standards  of  a\ngroup of susceptible or  sensitive  persons.</span>   The  Court  referred  to  the\npronouncement in Hicklin (supra) the majority view in Brody  v.  R[63],  and\nthe pronouncement in R. v. Butler[64] and opined thus:-\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_106\">\u201c23. We are also of the view that Hicklin test[65] is not the  correct  test\nto be applied to determine \u201cwhat is obscenity\u201d. Section  292  of  the  Penal\nCode, of course, uses the expression \u201clascivious and prurient interests\u201d  or\nits effect. Later, it has also been indicated in the  said  section  of  the\napplicability of the effect and the necessity  of  taking  the  items  as  a\nwhole and on that foundation where such items  would  tend  to  deprave  and\ncorrupt persons who are likely,  [pic]having  regard  to  all  the  relevant\ncircumstances, to read, see or hear the matter contained or embodied in  it.\nWe have, therefore, to apply the \u201ccommunity standard test\u201d rather  than  the\n\u201cHicklin test\u201d to determine what is \u201cobscenity\u201d.  A  bare  reading  of  sub-\nsection (1) of Section 292, makes clear that a picture or article  shall  be\ndeemed to be obscene\n\nif it is lascivious;\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_107\">(ii)  it appeals to the prurient interest; and\n\nit tends to deprave and corrupt persons who are likely to read, see or  hear\nthe matter, alleged to be obscene.\n</blockquote>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_75\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"561137\" id=\"span_36\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"1400858\" id=\"span_37\">Once the matter is found to  be  obscene,  the  question  may  arise  as  to\nwhether the impugned matter falls within any of the exceptions contained  in\nthe section. A picture of a nude/semi-nude woman, as such, cannot per se  be\ncalled obscene unless it has the  tendency  to  arouse  the  feeling  of  or\nrevealing an overt sexual  desire.  The  picture  should  be  suggestive  of\ndeprave mind and designed to  excite  sexual  passion  in  persons  who  are\nlikely to see it, which will  depend  on  the  particular  posture  and  the\nbackground in which the nude/semi-nude woman is depicted.  Only  those  sex-\nrelated materials which have a tendency of \u201cexciting lustful  thoughts\u201d  can\nbe held to be obscene, but the obscenity has to be judged from the point  of\nview of an average person, by applying contemporary community standards.\u201d\n\n      The Court also referred  to  <a href=\"/doc/1400858/\" id=\"a_134\">Bobby  Art  International</a>  (supra),  <a href=\"/doc/561137/\" id=\"a_135\">Ajay\nGoswami</a> (supra) and held that applying the  community  tolerance  test,  the\nphotograph was not suggestive  of  deprave  minds  and  designed  to  excite\nsexual passion in persons who are likely to look at them and see them.   The\nCourt further proceeded to state that the  photograph  has  no  tendency  to\ndeprave or corrupt the minds of the people because the said picture  has  to\nbe viewed in the background in which it was shown and the message it has  to\nconvey to the public and the world at large.  The Court observed that  Boris\nBecker himself in the article published in the  German  magazine,  spoke  of\nthe racial discrimination prevalent in Germany and the  article  highlighted\nBoris Becker\u2019s protest against racism in Germany.  Proceeding  further,  the\nCourt ruled that:-</span></span>\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_108\">\u201cThe message, the photograph wants to convey is  that  the  colour  of  skin\nmatters little and love champions over colour.  The  picture  promotes  love\naffair, leading to a marriage, between a white-skinned man and a [pic]black-\nskinned woman. We should,  therefore,  appreciate  the  photograph  and  the\narticle in the light  of  the  message  it  wants  to  convey,  that  is  to\neradicate the evil of racism and apartheid in the  society  and  to  promote\nlove and marriage between white-skinned man and a black-skinned woman.  When\nviewed in that angle, we are not prepared to say that  the  picture  or  the\narticle which was reproduced by Sports World and the Anandabazar Patrika  be\nsaid to be objectionable so as to initiate  proceedings  under  <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_136\">Section  292</a>\nIPC  or  under  <a href=\"/doc/124708675/\" id=\"a_137\">Section  4</a>  of  the   Indecent   Representation   of   Women\n(Prohibition) Act, 1986.\u201d\n\n      Thus, the  aforesaid  decision  applies  the  \u201ccontemporary  community\nstandards test\u201d and rules that the factum of  obscenity  has  to  be  judged\nfrom the point of view of an average person.\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_109\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"110813550\" id=\"span_38\">59.   Very recently, in <a href=\"/doc/110813550/\" id=\"a_138\">Shreya Singhal v. Union of  India</a>[66],  a  two-Judge\nBench of this Court, while dealing with the concept of obscenity,  has  held\nthat:-</span>\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_110\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"1623275\" id=\"span_39\">\u201c45.  This Court in <a href=\"/doc/1623275/\" id=\"a_139\">Ranjit Udeshi</a> (supra) took a rather restrictive view  of\nwhat would pass muster as not being obscene.  The Court  followed  the  test\n<a href=\"/doc/1623275/\" id=\"a_140\">laid down in</a> the old English judgment in Hicklin\u2019s case  which  was  whether\nthe tendency of the matter charged as obscene  is  to  deprave  and  corrupt\nthose whose minds are open to such immoral influences and into who  hands  a\npublication of this sort may fall.  Great strides have been made since  this\ndecision in UK, United  States,  as  well  as  in  our  country.</span>   <span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"206282\" id=\"span_40\">Thus,  in\n<a href=\"/doc/206282/\" id=\"a_141\">Director General of Doordarshan v. We have referred to the concept of obscenity as has been put forth  by\nthe learned senior counsel for the appellant, the prevalent test  in  United\nKingdom, United States of America and the test formulated  by  the  European\nCourts.  We have extensively dealt with the test adopted  in  this  country.\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_76\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"149399940\" id=\"span_42\">On the studied scrutiny and analysis of  the  judgments,  there  can  be  no\nshadow of doubt that this Court has laid down various guidelines  from  time\nto time and accepted  the  contemporary  community  standards  test  as  the\nparameter and also observed that the contemporary community  standards  test\nwould vary from time to time, for the perception, views,  ideas  and  ideals\ncan never remain static.  They have to move with  time  and  development  of\nculture. We  respectfully  concur  with  the\nsaid view and hold that contemporary community standards test  is  the  main\ncriterion and  it  has  to  be  appreciated  on  the  foundation  of  modern\nperception, regard being had to the criterion that develops the  literature.\n There can neither be stagnation of ideas nor  there  can  be  staticity  of\nideals.  The innovative minds can conceive of many a thing and project  them\nin different ways.   As far as comparables test is concerned, the Court  may\nsometimes have referred to  various  books  on  literature  of  the  foreign\nauthors and expressed the view that certain writings are  not  obscene,  but\nthat is not the applicable test.  It may at best reflect what the  community\naccepts.</span>\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_77\">Right to Freedom of Speech and Expression under the Constitution\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_78\">61. We do not intend to say that a poem should conform to  the  definition\nor description as many authors have thought of.   According  to  Dr.  Samuel\nJohnson, \u201cPoetry is \u2018metrical  composition\u2019;  it  is  \u2018the  art  of  uniting\npleasure with truth by calling imagination to the help of reason\u2019;  and  its\n\u2018essence\u2019 is \u2018invention\u2019.\u201d\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_123\">96.   Mill\u2019s point of view \u201cpoetry is, but the thought and  words  in  which\nemotion spontaneously embodies itself.\u201d  Macaulay understands poetry as  \u201cwe\nmean the art of employing words in such a manner as to produce  an  illusion\non the imagination, the art of doing by means  of  words  what  the  painter\ndoes by means of colours\u201d.[128]\n</p><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_124\">97.   Carlyle assumed that poetry is \u201cwe will  call  Musical  Thought\u201d.[129]\nShelley states, \u201cin a general sense may be defined as the expression of  the\nimagination\u201d.[130]  Hazlitt defines poetry as \u201cit is  the  language  of  the\nimagination and the passions\u201d.[131]\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_125\">98.   Leigh Hunt declares poetry as \u201cthe utterance of a passion  for  truth,\nbeauty,  and  power,  embodying  and   illustrating   its   conceptions   by\nimagination and fancy, and modulating  its  language  on  the  principle  of\nvariety in unity\u201d.[132]\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_126\">99.   S.T. Coleridge\u2019s has expressed  that  poetry  is  the  anti-thesis  of\nscience, having for its immediate object pleasure, not  truth.[133]   German\nphilosopher Hegel has thought that the use of verse  in  a  given  piece  of\nliterature serves  in  itself  to  lift  the  mankind  into  a  world  quite\ndifferent from that of prose or everyday life.  Emerson says that the  great\npoets are judged by the  frame  of  mind  they  induce.[134]   There  is  no\ndifficulty in saying that the definition  or  understanding  of  concept  of\npoetry of any high authority can be ignored.  That is  the  freedom  of  the\npoem.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_127\">The poem in issue\n</p><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_128\">100.  Presently, to the poem in  question  we  are  referring  to  the  same\nsolely for the purpose of adjudging whether the order of framing  of  charge\nunder  <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_216\">Section  292</a>  IPC  is  sustainable,  regard  being  had  to  the  law\npertaining to charge, and whether the High Court has correctly  applied  the\nprinciple. The principle pertaining to the freedom of speech has to  be\ninterpreted on an extremely broad canvas and  under  no  circumstances,  any\nhistorical personality can cause an impediment in the  same.   It  is  urged\nthat the Constitution of India is  an  impersonalised  document  and  poetry\nwhich encourages fearlessness of expression, cannot  be  restricted  because\nof use of name of  a  personality.    Learned  senior  counsel  has  further\nsubmitted that freedom to offend is  also  a  part  of  freedom  of  speech.\nPoetry, which  is  a  great  liberator,  submits  Mr.  Subramanium,  can  be\ncomposed through a merely voice explaining plurality of thought.   He  would\nsubmit  the  instant  poem  is  one  where   there   is   \u201ctransference   of\nconsciousness\u201d that exposes the social hypocrisy and it cannot be  perceived\nwith a conditioned mind.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_132\">104.  The principle that has been  put  forth  by  Mr.  Subramanium  can  be\nbroadly accepted, but we do not intend to express any opinion  that  freedom\nof speech gives liberty to offend.   As far  as  the  use  of  the  name  of\nhistorically respected personality is  concerned,  learned  senior  counsel,\nwhile submitting so, is making an endeavour to put the freedom of speech  on\nthe pedestal of an absolute concept.  Freedom of speech and  expression  has\nto be given a broad canvas, but it has to have  inherent  limitations  which\nare permissible within the  constitutional  parameters.    We  have  already\nopined that freedom of speech and  expression  as  enshrined  under  <a href=\"/doc/1378441/\" id=\"a_221\">Article\n19(1)(a)</a> of the Constitution is not absolute in view  of  <a href=\"/doc/493243/\" id=\"a_222\">Article  19(2)</a>  of\nthe Constitution.  We reiterate the said right is a  right  of  great  value\nand transcends and with the passage of time and growth of  culture,  it  has\nto pave the path of ascendancy, but it cannot be put in the  compartment  of\nabsoluteness.  There is constitutional limitation attached to  it.   In  the\ncontext of obscenity, the provision enshrined under <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_223\">Section 292</a> IPC has  its\nroom to play.  We have already opined that by  bringing  in  a  historically\nrespected personality to the  arena  of  <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_224\">Section  292</a>  IPC,  neither  a  new\noffence is  created  nor  an  ingredient  is  interpreted.   The  judicially\nevolved  test,  that  is,  \u201ccontemporary  community  standards  test\u201d  is  a\nparameter for adjudging obscenity, and in that context, the  words  used  or\nspoken by a historically respected personality is a medium of  communication\nthrough  a  poem  or  write-up  or  other  form  of   artistic   work   gets\nsignification.  That makes the test applicable  in  a  greater  degree.   To\nunderstand the  same,  a  concrete  example  can  be  given.   A  playwright\nconceives a plot where Mahatma  Gandhi,  Vishwakavi  Rabindra  Nath  Tagore,\nSardar Vallabh Bhai Patel meet in heaven and they engage themselves  in  the\ndiscussion of their activities what they had undertaken when they  lived  in\ntheir human frame.  In course of discussion, their conversation enters  into\nthe  area  of  egoism,  thereafter  slowly  graduates  into  the  sphere  of\nmegalomania and eventually they start abusing each other and in  the  abuses\nthey use obscene words.  The question would be  whether  the  dramatist  can\ncontend that he has used them as symbolic voices to echo the idea  of  human\nfallacy and it\u2019s a creation  of  his  imagination;  and  creativity  has  no\nlimitation and, therefore, there is no obscenity.  But, there is a  pregnant\none, the author has chosen historically respected persons as medium  to  put\ninto their  mouth  obscene  words  and,  ergo,  the  creativity  melts  into\ninsignificance and obscenity merges into surface even if  he  had  chosen  a\n\u201ctarget domain\u201d. He  in  his  approach  has  travelled  into  the  field  of\nperversity and moved away from the permissible \u201ctarget domain\u201d, for  in  the\ncontext the historically respected personality matters.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_133\">Conclusion\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_134\">105.  When the name of Mahatma Gandhi  is  alluded  or  used  as  a  symbol,\nspeaking or using obscene words, the  concept  of  \u201cdegree\u201d  comes  in.   To\nelaborate, the \u201ccontemporary community standards  test\u201d  becomes  applicable\nwith more vigour, in a greater degree and in an  accentuated  manner.   What\ncan otherwise pass of the contemporary community standards test for  use  of\nthe same language, it would not be so, if the  name  of  Mahatma  Gandhi  is\nused as a symbol or allusion or surrealistic voice to put words or  to  show\nhim doing such acts which are obscene.  While so concluding, we leave it  to\nthe poet to put his defense at the trial explaining the manner he  has  used\nthe words and in what context.  We only opine that view of  the  High  Court\npertaining to the framing of charge under <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_225\">Section 292</a> IPC cannot be  flawed.\n</p>\n\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_135\">106.  Coming to the  case  put  forth  by  the  appellant-publisher,  it  is\nnoticeable that he had published the poem in  question,  which  had  already\nbeen recited during the Akhil Bhartiya Sahithya Sammelan at  Amba  Jogai  in\n1980, and was earlier published on 2.10.1986 by others.  The  appellant  has\npublished the poem only in 1994.   But  immediately  after  coming  to  know\nabout the reactions of certain employees, he tendered unconditional  apology\nin  the  next  issue  of  the  \u2018Bulletin\u2019.   Once  he   has   tendered   the\nunconditional apology even before  the  inception  of  the  proceedings  and\nalmost more than two decades have passed,  we  are  inclined  to  quash  the\ncharge framed against him as well as the printer.  We are disposed to  quash\nthe charge against the printer, as it is submitted that he  had  printed  as\ndesired by the  publisher.   Hence,  they  stand  discharged.   However,  we\nrepeat at the cost of repetition that we have not expressed any  opinion  as\nto the act on the part of the author of the poem, who is co-accused  in  the\ncase, and facing trial before the  Magistrate  in  respect  of  the  offence\npunishable under <a href=\"/doc/1704109/\" id=\"a_226\">Section 292</a> IPC.  It shall be open for  him  to  raise  all\nthe pleas in defence, as available to him under the law.  At this  juncture,\nwe are obliged to mention that Mr. Nariman,  learned  friend  of  the  Court\nalso in course of hearing, had submitted that the appellant  having  offered\nunconditional apology immediately and regard being had  to  the  passage  of\ntime, he along with the printer should be discharged.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_136\">107.  Before we part with the case, we must candidly express our  unreserved\nand uninhibited appreciation for the distinguished  assistance  rendered  by\nMr.  Fali  S.  Nariman,  learned  amicus  curiae.   We   also   record   our\nappreciation for the sustained  endeavour  put  forth  by  Mr.  Subramanium,\nlearned senior counsel for the appellant, for it has been of  immense  value\nin rendering the judgment.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_137\">108.
