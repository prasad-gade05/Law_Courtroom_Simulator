The present batch of Writ Petitions raise an Orwellian\n\nconcern, about the alleged possibility of utilizing modern\n\ntechnology to hear what you hear, see what you see and to know\n\nwhat you do. In this context, this Court is called upon to\n\nexamine an allegation of the use of such a technology, its utility,\n\nneed and alleged abuse. We make it clear that our effort is to\n\nuphold the constitutional aspirations and rule of law, without\n\nallowing ourselves to be consumed in the political rhetoric. This\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_1\">                                 2</span>\n\fCourt has always been conscious of not entering the political\n\nthicket. However, at the same time, it has never cowered from\n\nprotecting all from the abuses of fundamental rights. All that we\n\nwould like to observe in this regard is a reiteration of what had\n\nalready been said by this Court in Kesavananda Bharati v.\n\nState of Kerala, (Opinion of Justice Khanna) AIR 1973 SC 1461:\n\n\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_1\">     \u201c1535\u2026. Judicial review is not intended to create what\n     is sometimes called judicial oligarchy, the aristrocracy\n     (sic) of the robe, covert legislation, or Judge\u00admade law.\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_3\">     The proper forum to fight for the wise use of the\n     legislative authority is that of public opinion and\n     legislative assemblies. Such contest cannot be\n     transferred to the judicial arena. That all\n     constitutional      interpretations       have   political\n     consequences should not obliterate the fact that the\n     decision has to be arrived at in the calm and\n     dispassionate atmosphere of the court room, that\n     Judges in order to give legitimacy to their decision\n     have to keep aloof from the din and controversy of\n     politics and that the fluctuating fortunes of rival\n     political parties can have for them only academic\n     interest. Their primary duty is to uphold the\n     Constitution and the laws without fear or favour and\n     in doing so, they cannot allow any political ideology or\n     economic theory, which may have caught their fancy,\n     to colour the decision\u2026\u201d\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Facts\" id=\"p_4\">2.   A short conspectus of the events leading up to the present\n\nbatch of petitions would not be misplaced to highlight the scope\n\nof the issues at hand. In September 2018, Citizen Lab, which is a\n\nlaboratory based out of the University of Toronto, Canada,\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_2\">                                 3</span>\n\freleased a report detailing the software capabilities of a \u201cspyware\n\nsuite\u201d called Pegasus that was being produced by an Israeli\n\nTechnology firm, viz., the NSO Group. The report indicated that\n\nindividuals from nearly 45 countries were suspected to have been\n\naffected.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_5\">3.   The Pegasus suite of spywares can allegedly be used to\n\ncompromise the digital devices of an individual through zero click\n\nvulnerabilities, i.e., without requiring any action on the part of\n\nthe target of the software. Once the software infiltrates an\n\nindividual\u2019s device, it allegedly has the capacity to access the\n\nentire stored data on the device, and has real time access to\n\nemails, texts, phone calls, as well as the camera and sound\n\nrecording capabilities of the device. Once the device is infiltrated\n\nusing Pegasus, the entire control over the device is allegedly\n\nhanded over to the Pegasus user who can then remotely control\n\nall the functionalities of the device and switch different features\n\non or off. The NSO Group purportedly sells this extremely\n\npowerful software only to certain undisclosed Governments and\n\nthe end user of its products are \u201cexclusively government\n\nintelligence and law enforcement agencies\u201d as per its own\n\nwebsite.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Issue\" id=\"p_6\">\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_3\">                                 4</span>\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_7\">\f4.   In May 2019, the global messaging giant WhatsApp Inc.\n\nidentified a vulnerability in its software that enabled Pegasus\n\nspyware to infiltrate the devices of WhatsApp\u2019s users. This news\n\nwas followed by a disclosure that the devices of certain Indians\n\nwere also affected, which fact was acknowledged by the then\n\nHon\u2019ble Minister of Law and Electronics and Information\n\nTechnology in a statement made in the Parliament on 20 th\n\nNovember 2019.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Issue\" id=\"p_8\">5.   On 15th June 2020, Citizen Lab, in collaboration with the\n\ninternational human rights organization, Amnesty International\n\nuncovered another spyware campaign which allegedly targeted\n\nnine individuals in India, some of whom were already suspected\n\ntargets in the first spyware attack.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Facts\" id=\"p_9\">6.   On 18th July 2021, a consortium of nearly 17 journalistic\n\norganizations from around the world, including one Indian\n\norganization, released the results of a long investigative effort\n\nindicating the alleged use of the Pegasus software on several\n\nprivate individuals. This investigative effort was based on a list of\n\nsome 50,000 leaked numbers which were allegedly under\n\nsurveillance by clients of the NSO Group through the Pegasus\n\nsoftware. Initially, it was discovered that nearly 300 of these\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_4\">                                  5</span>\n\fnumbers belonged to Indians, many of whom are senior\n\njournalists, doctors, political persons, and even some Court staff.\n\nAt the time of filing of the Writ Petitions, nearly 10 Indians\u2019\n\ndevices were allegedly forensically analyzed to confirm the\n\npresence of the Pegasus software.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_10\">7.   The above reports resulted in largescale action across the\n\nglobe, with certain foreign governments even diplomatically\n\nengaging with the Israeli Government to determine the veracity of\n\nthe allegations raised, while other governments have initiated\n\nproceedings internally to determine the truth of the same.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_11\">8.   Respondent\u00adUnion of India, through the Hon\u2019ble Minister of\n\nRailways, Communications and Electronics and Information\n\nTechnology, took the stand in Parliament on 18 th July 2021,\n\nwhen asked about the alleged cyberattack and spyware use, that\n\nthe reports published had no factual basis. Finally, he stated that\n\nthe Indian statutory and legal regime relating to surveillance and\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_5\">                                 6</span>\n\finterception of communication is extremely rigorous, and no\n\nillegal surveillance could take place.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_12\">9. At the outset, certain nuances of the right to privacy in\n\nIndia\u00ad its facets and importance, need to be discussed.\n\nHistorically, privacy rights have been \u2018property centric\u2019 rather\n\nthan people centric. This approach was seen in both the United\n\nStates of America as well as in England. In 1604, in the historical\n\nSemayne\u2019s case, 77 ER 194 (KB) it was famously held that\n\n\u201cevery man\u2019s house is his castle\u201d. This marked the beginning of\n\nthe development of the law protecting people against unlawful\n\nwarrants and searches.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_31\">28. As William Pitt, the Earl of Chatham stated in March 1763 1:\n\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_7\">        \u201cThe poorest man may in his cottage bid defiance to all\n        the force of the Crown. It may be frail\u2014its roof may\n        shake\u2014the wind may blow through it\u2014the storm may\n        enter, the rain may enter\u2014but the King of England\n        cannot enter!\u2014all his force dares not cross the\n        threshold of the ruined tenement!\u201d\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_16\">1</span>\n Lord Brougham, Historical Sketches of Statesmen who Flourished in the Time of George III First Series,\nVol. 1 (1845).\n</blockquote>\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_17\">                                                   17</span>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_32\">\f29. As long back as in 1890, Samuel Warren and Louis\n\nBrandeis observed in their celebrated article \u2018The Right to\n\nPrivacy\u20192:\n</p>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_8\">        \u201cRecent inventions and business methods call\n        attention to the next step which must be taken for the\n        protection of the person, and for securing to the\n        individual what Judge Cooley calls the right \u201cto be let\n        alone.\u201d\u2026numerous mechanical devices threaten to\n        make good the prediction that \u201cwhat is whispered in\n        the closet shall be proclaimed from the house\u00adtops.\u201d\n\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_33\">30. However, unlike the \u2018property centric\u2019 origin of privacy\n\nrights in England and under the Fourth Amendment in the\n\nConstitution of the United States of America, in India, privacy\n\nrights may be traced to the \u2018right to life\u2019 enshrined under <a href=\"/doc/1199182/\" id=\"a_1\">Article\n\n21</a> of the Constitution. When this Court expounded on the\n\nmeaning of \u201clife\u201d under <a href=\"/doc/1199182/\" id=\"a_2\">Article 21</a>, it did not restrict the same in a\n\npedantic manner. An expanded meaning has been given to the\n\nright to life in India, which accepts that \u201clife\u201d does not refer to\n\nmere animal existence but encapsulates a certain assured\n\nquality.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Issue\" id=\"p_34\">31. It is in this context that we must contextualize the issues\n\nthat are being raised in this batch of petitions. We live in the era\n\nof information revolution, where the entire lives of individuals are\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_18\">2</span>\n Samuel Warren and Louis Brandeis, The Right to Privacy, HARVARD LAW REVIEW, Vol. 4 (5), 193 (Dec. 15,\n1890).\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_35\">\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_19\">                                                  18</span>\n\fstored in the cloud or in a digital dossier. We must recognize that\n\nwhile technology is a useful tool for improving the lives of the\n\npeople, at the same time, it can also be used to breach that\n\nsacred private space of an individual.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_36\">32. Members of a civilized democratic society have a reasonable\n\nexpectation of privacy. Privacy is not the singular concern of\n\njournalists or social activists. Every citizen of India ought to be\n\nprotected against violations of privacy. It is this expectation\n\nwhich enables us to exercise our choices, liberties, and freedom.\n\nThis Court in K.S. Puttaswamy (Privacy\u00ad9J.) v. Union of India,\n\n(2017) 10 SCC 1, has recognized that the right to privacy is as\n\nsacrosanct as human existence and is inalienable to human\n\ndignity and autonomy. This Court held that:\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_37\">\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_9\">     \u201c320. Privacy is a constitutionally protected right\n     which emerges primarily from the guarantee of life\n     and personal liberty in <a href=\"/doc/1199182/\" id=\"a_3\">Article 21</a> of the\n     Constitution. Elements of privacy also arise in\n     varying contexts from the other facets of freedom and\n     dignity recognised and guaranteed by the fundamental\n     rights contained in Part III.\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_10\">     \u2026\n\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_11\">     325. Like other rights which form part of the\n     fundamental freedoms protected by Part III, including\n     the right to life and personal liberty under <a href=\"/doc/1199182/\" id=\"a_4\">Article 21</a>,\n     privacy is not an absolute right. A law which\n     encroaches upon privacy will have to withstand the\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_20\">                                19</span>\n\f      touchstone    of    permissible    restrictions  on\n      fundamental rights. In the context of <a href=\"/doc/1199182/\" id=\"a_5\">Article 21</a> an\n      invasion of privacy must be justified on the basis\n      of a law which stipulates a procedure which is fair,\n      just and reasonable. The law must also be valid\n      with reference to the encroachment on life and\n      personal liberty under <a href=\"/doc/1199182/\" id=\"a_6\">Article 21.</a> An invasion of\n      life or personal liberty must meet the threefold\n      requirement of (i) legality, which postulates the\n      existence of law; (ii) need, defined in terms of a\n      legitimate State aim; and (iii) proportionality\n      which ensures a rational nexus between the objects\n      and the means adopted to achieve them.\u201d\n                                      (emphasis supplied)\n\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_38\">33. Although declared to be inalienable, the right to privacy of\n\ncourse cannot be said to be an absolute, as the Indian\n\nConstitution does not provide for such a right without reasonable\n\nrestrictions. As with all the other fundamental rights, this Court\n\ntherefore must recognize that certain limitations exist when it\n\ncomes to the right to privacy as well. However, any restrictions\n\nimposed must necessarily pass constitutional scrutiny.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_39\">34.    In K.S. Puttaswamy (Privacy\u00ad9J.) (supra), this Court\n\nconsidered the need to protect the privacy interests of individuals\n\nwhile furthering legitimate State interests. This Court therefore\n\ndirected the State to embark upon the exercise of balancing of\n\ncompeting interests. This Court observed as follows:\n\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_12\">      \u201c310.While it intervenes to protect legitimate\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_21\">                                20</span>\n\fState interests, the State must nevertheless put\ninto place a robust regime that ensures the\nfulfilment of a threefold requirement. These three\nrequirements apply to all restraints on privacy (not\njust informational privacy). They emanate from the\nprocedural and content\u00adbased mandate of Article\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_13\">21. The first requirement that there must be a law in\nexistence to justify an encroachment on privacy is an\nexpress requirement of <a href=\"/doc/1199182/\" id=\"a_7\">Article 21.</a> For, no person can\nbe deprived of his life or personal liberty except in\naccordance with the procedure established by law. The\nexistence of law is an essential requirement. Second,\nthe requirement of a need, in terms of a legitimate\nState aim, ensures that the nature and content of the\nlaw which imposes the restriction falls within the zone\nof reasonableness mandated by <a href=\"/doc/367586/\" id=\"a_8\">Article 14</a>, which is a\nguarantee against arbitrary State action. The pursuit\nof a legitimate State aim ensures that the law does not\nsuffer from manifest arbitrariness. Legitimacy, as a\npostulate, involves a value judgment. Judicial review\ndoes not reappreciate or second guess the value\njudgment of the legislature but is for deciding whether\nthe aim which is sought to be pursued suffers from\npalpable or manifest arbitrariness. The third\nrequirement ensures that the means which are\nadopted by the legislature are proportional to the\nobject and needs sought to be fulfilled by the law.\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_40\">Proportionality is an essential facet of the\nguarantee against arbitrary State action because it\nensures that the nature and quality of the\nencroachment on the right is not disproportionate\nto the purpose of the law. Hence, the threefold\nrequirement for a valid law arises out of the mutual\ninterdependence        between     the     fundamental\nguarantees against arbitrariness on the one hand\nand the protection of life and personal liberty, on\nthe other. The right to privacy, which is an\nintrinsic part of the right to life and liberty, and\nthe freedoms embodied in Part III is subject to the\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_22\">                          21</span>\n\f          same restraints which apply to those freedoms.\u201d\n                                         (emphasis supplied)\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_41\">35. The right to privacy is directly infringed when there is\n\nsurveillance or spying done on an individual, either by the State\n\nor by any external agency. Ellen Alderman and Caroline\n\nKennedy, in \u2018Right to Privacy\u2019, 3 foresaw this threat to privacy in\n\n1995, while referring to governmental eavesdropping in the\n\nUnited States of America, in the following words:\n\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_14\">          \u201cPerhaps the scariest threat to privacy comes in the\n          area known as \u201cinformational privacy\u201d. Information\n          about all of us is now collected not only by the old\n          standbys, the IRS and FBI, but also by the MTB, MIB,\n          NCOA, and NCIC, as well as credit bureaus, credit\n          unions, and credit card companies. We now have\n          cellular phones, which are different from cordless\n          phones, which are different from what we used to\n          think of as phones. We worry about e\u00admail, voice mail,\n          and junk mail. And something with the perky name\n          Clipper Chip \u00ad developed specifically to allow\n          governmental eavesdropping on coded electronic\n          communications \u2013 is apparently the biggest threat of\n          all.\u201d\n\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_42\">36. Of course, if done by the State, the same must be justified\n\non constitutional grounds. This Court is cognizant of the State\u2019s\n\ninterest to ensure that life and liberty is preserved and must\n\nbalance the same. For instance, in today\u2019s world, information\n\ngathered           by     intelligence         agencies         through      surveillance   is\n\nessential for the fight against violence and terror. To access this\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_23\">3</span>\n    Ellen Alderman and Caroline Kennedy, THE RIGHT TO PRIVACY, 223 (1995).\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_24\">                                                    22</span>\n\finformation, a need may arise to interfere with the right to\n\nprivacy of an individual, provided it is carried out only when it is\n\nabsolutely necessary for protecting national security/interest and\n\nis proportional. The considerations for usage of such alleged\n\ntechnology, ought to be evidence based. In a democratic country\n\ngoverned by the rule of law, indiscriminate spying on individuals\n\ncannot be allowed except with sufficient statutory safeguards, by\n\nfollowing      the     procedure            established         by      law       under        the\n\nConstitution.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_43\">37. This trade\u00adoff between the right to privacy of an individual\n\nand the security interests of the State, has been recognized world\n\nover with the renowned scholar Daniel Solove4 commenting on\n\nthe same as follows:\n</p>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_15\">       \u201cThe debate between privacy and security has been\n       framed incorrectly, with the trade\u00adoff between these\n       values understood as an all\u00ador\u00adnothing proposition.\n       But protecting privacy need not be fatal to security\n       measures; it merely demands oversight and\n       regulation. We can\u2019t progress in the debate\n       between privacy and security because the debate\n       itself is flawed.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_16\">       The law suffers from related problems. It seeks to\n       balance privacy and security, but systematic problems\n       plague the way the balancing takes place\u2026.\n</blockquote>\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_25\">4</span>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_17\">  Daniel J. Solove, NOTHING   TO   HIDE: THE FALSE TRADEOFF   BETWEEN   PRIVACY   AND   SECURITY\n(2011).\n</blockquote>\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_26\">                                                23</span>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_44\">\f     Privacy often can be protected without undue cost to\n     security. In instances when adequate compromises\n     can\u2019t be achieved, the trade\u00adoff can be made in a\n     manner that is fair to both sides. We can reach a\n     better balance between privacy and security. We must.\n     There is too much at stake to fail.\u201d\n                                        (emphasis supplied)\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_45\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"223504\" id=\"span_27\">38. Somewhat allied to the concerns of privacy, is the freedom\n\nof the press. Certain observations made by this Court in the case\n\nof <a href=\"/doc/223504/\" id=\"a_9\">Indian Express Newspapers (Bombay) Private Limited v.\n\nUnion of India</a>, (1985) 1 SCC 641 may be extracted:</span>\n\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_18\">     \u201c25. The freedom of press, as one of the members of\n     the Constituent Assembly said, is one of the items\n     around which the greatest and the bitterest of\n     constitutional struggles have been waged in all\n     countries where liberal constitutions prevail. The said\n     freedom is attained at considerable sacrifice and\n     suffering and ultimately it has come to be incorporated\n     in the various written constitutions\u2026\u201d\n\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_46\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"82461587\" id=\"span_28\">39. It is undeniable that surveillance and the knowledge that\n\none is under the threat of being spied on can affect the way an\n\nindividual decides to exercise his or her rights. Such a scenario\n\nmight result in self\u00adcensorship. This is of particular concern\n\nwhen it relates to the freedom of the press, which is an important\n\npillar of democracy. Such chilling effect on the freedom of speech\n\nis an assault on the vital public\u00adwatchdog role of the press,\n\nwhich may undermine the ability of the press to provide accurate\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_29\">                                24</span>\n\fand reliable information. Recently, in the case of <a href=\"/doc/82461587/\" id=\"a_10\">Anuradha\n\nBhasin v. Union of India</a>, (2020) 3 SCC 637, this Court\n\nhighlighted the importance of freedom of the press in a modern\n\ndemocracy in the following words:</span>\n</p>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_19\">     \u201c159. In this context, one possible test of chilling\n     effect is comparative harm. In this framework, the\n     Court is required to see whether the impugned\n     restrictions, due to their broad\u00adbased nature, have\n     had a restrictive effect on similarly placed\n     individuals during the period. It is the contention of\n     the petitioner that she was not able to publish her\n     newspaper from 6\u00ad8\u00ad2019 to 11\u00ad10\u00ad2019. However, no\n     evidence was put forth to establish that such other\n     individuals were also restricted in publishing\n     newspapers in the area. Without such evidence having\n     been placed on record, it would be impossible to\n     distinguish a legitimate claim of chilling effect from a\n     mere emotive argument for a self\u00adserving purpose. On\n     the other hand, the learned Solicitor General has\n     submitted that there were other newspapers which\n     were running during the aforesaid time period. In view\n     of these facts, and considering that the aforesaid\n     petitioner has now resumed publication, we do not\n     deem it fit to indulge more in the issue than to state\n     that responsible Governments are required to respect\n     the freedom of the press at all times. Journalists are\n     to be accommodated in reporting and there is no\n     justification for allowing a sword of Damocles to\n     hang over the press indefinitely.\u201d\n                                       (emphasis supplied)\n\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_47\">40. An important and necessary corollary of such a right is to\n\nensure the protection of sources of information. Protection of\n\njournalistic sources is one of the basic conditions for the freedom\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_30\">                                25</span>\n\fof the press. Without such protection, sources may be deterred\n\nfrom assisting the press in informing the public on matters of\n\npublic interest.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_48\">41. Having regard to the importance of the protection of\n\njournalistic sources for press freedom in a democratic society\n\nand the potential chilling effect that snooping techniques may\n\nhave, this Court\u2019s task in the present matter, where certain grave\n\nallegations of infringement of the rights of the citizens of the\n\ncountry have been raised, assumes great significance. In this\n\nlight, this Court is compelled to take up the cause to determine\n\nthe truth and get to the bottom of the allegations made herein.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_49\">42. Initially, this Court was not satisfied with the Writ Petitions\n\nthat were filed as the same were completely reliant only upon\n\ncertain newspaper reports. This Court has generally attempted to\n\ndiscourage Writ Petitions, particularly Public Interest Litigations,\n\nwhich are based entirely on newspaper reports without any\n\nadditional steps taken by the Petitioner. In this respect, it may be\n\nrelevant to quote the observations of this Court in the case of\n\nRohit Pandey v. Union of India, (2005) 13 SCC 702, which are\n\nas follows:\n</p>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_20\">     \u201c1. \u2026The only basis for the petitioner coming to\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_31\">                                 26</span>\n\f     this Court are two newspaper reports dated\n     25\u00ad1\u00ad2004, and the other dated 12\u00ad2\u00ad2004. This\n     petition was immediately filed on 16\u00ad2\u00ad2004 after the\n     aforesaid second newspaper report appeared\u2026.\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_21\">     2. We expect that when such a petition is filed in\n     public interest and particularly by a member of the\n     legal profession, it would be filed with all\n     seriousness and after doing the necessary\n     homework and enquiry. If the petitioner is so public\u00ad\n     spirited at such a young age as is so professed, the\n     least one would expect is that an enquiry would be\n     made from the authorities concerned as to the nature\n     of investigation which may be going on before filing a\n     petition that the investigation be conducted by the\n     Central Bureau of Investigation. Admittedly, no such\n     measures were taken by the petitioner. There is\n     nothing in the petition as to what, in fact, prompted\n     the petitioner to approach this Court within two\u00adthree\n     days of the second publication dated 12\u00ad2\u00ad2004, in the\n     newspaper Amar Ujala. Further, the State of Uttar\n     Pradesh had filed its affidavit a year earlier i.e. on 7\u00ad\n     10\u00ad2004, placing on record the steps taken against the\n     accused persons, including the submission of the\n     charge\u00adsheet before the appropriate court. Despite one\n     year having elapsed after the filing of the affidavit by\n     the Special Secretary to the Home Department of the\n     Government of Uttar Pradesh, nothing seems to have\n     been done by the petitioner. The petitioner has not\n     even controverted what is stated in the affidavit.\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_50\">     Ordinarily, we would have dismissed such a\n     misconceived petition with exemplary costs but\n     considering that the petitioner is a young advocate, we\n     feel that the ends of justice would be met and the\n     necessary message conveyed if a token cost of rupees\n     one thousand is imposed on the petitioner.\u201d\n                                      (emphasis supplied)\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_51\">43. While we understand that the allegations made in these\n\npetitions pertain to matters about which ordinary citizens would\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_32\">                                27</span>\n\fnot have information except for the investigating reporting done\n\nby news agencies, looking to the quality of some of the petitions\n\nfiled, we are constrained to observe that individuals should not\n\nfile half\u00adbaked petitions merely on a few newspaper reports. Such\n\nan exercise, far from helping the cause espoused by the\n\nindividual filing the petition, is often detrimental to the cause\n\nitself. This is because the Court will not have proper assistance\n\nin the matter, with the burden to even determine preliminary\n\nfacts being left to the Court. It is for this reason that trigger\n\nhappy filing of such petitions in Courts, and more particularly in\n\nthis Court which is to be the final adjudicatory body in the\n\ncountry, needs to be discouraged. This should not be taken to\n\nmean that the news agencies are not trusted by the Court, but to\n\nemphasize the role that each pillar of democracy occupies in the\n\npolity. News agencies report facts and bring to light issues which\n\nmight otherwise not be publicly known. These may then become\n\nthe basis for further action taken by an active and concerned\n\ncivil society, as well as for any subsequent filings made in\n\nCourts. But newspaper reports, in and of themselves, should not\n\nin the ordinary course be taken to be ready\u00admade pleadings that\n\nmay be filed in Court.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_52\">\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_33\">                                28</span>\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_53\">\f44. That said, after we indicated our reservations to the\n\nPetitioners regarding the lack of material, various other petitions\n\nhave been filed in Court, including by individuals who were\n\npurportedly victims of the alleged Pegasus spyware attack. These\n\nsubsequently filed petitions, as well as additional documents\n\nfiled by others, have brought on record certain materials that\n\ncannot be brushed aside, such as the reports of reputed\n\norganizations     like   Citizen        Lab    and   affidavits   of   experts.\n\nAdditionally, the sheer volume of cross\u00adreferenced and cross\u00ad\n\nverified reports from various reputable news organizations across\n\nthe world along with the reactions of foreign governments and\n\nlegal institutions also moved us to consider that this is a case\n\nwhere the jurisdiction of the Court may be exercised. Of course,\n\nthe learned Solicitor General suggested that many of these\n\nreports are motivated and self\u00adserving. However, such an\n\nomnibus    oral    allegation      is    not    sufficient   to   desist   from\n\ninterference.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_54\">45. It is for this reason that this Court issued notice to the\n\nRespondent\u00adUnion of India and sought information from them.\n\nWe would like to re\u00ademphasize what is already apparent from the\n\nrecord of proceedings. This Court gave ample opportunity to the\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_34\">                                         29</span>\n\fRespondent\u00adUnion of India to clarify its stand regarding the\n\nallegations raised, and to provide information to assist the Court\n\nregarding the various actions taken by it over the past two years,\n\nsince the first disclosed alleged Pegasus spyware attack. We had\n\nmade it clear to the learned Solicitor General on many occasions\n\nthat we would not push the Respondent\u00adUnion of India to provide\n\nany information that may affect the national security concerns of\n\nthe country. However, despite the repeated assurances and\n\nopportunities given, ultimately the Respondent\u00adUnion of India\n\nhas placed on record what they call a \u201climited affidavit\u201d, which\n\ndoes not shed any light on their stand or provide any clarity as to\n\nthe facts of the matter at hand. If the Respondent\u00adUnion of India\n\nhad made their stand clear it would have been a different\n\nsituation, and the burden on us would have been different.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_55\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"1232445\" id=\"span_35\">46. Such a course of action taken by the Respondent\u00adUnion of\n\nIndia, especially in proceedings of the present nature which\n\ntouches upon the fundamental rights of the citizens of the\n\ncountry, cannot be accepted. As held by this Court in <a href=\"/doc/1232445/\" id=\"a_11\">Ram\n\nJethmalani    v.   Union   of   India</a>,   (2011)   8   SCC   1,   the\n\nRespondent\u00adUnion of India should not take an adversarial\n\nposition when the fundamental rights of citizens are at threat.\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_36\">                                30</span>\n\fThis Court <a href=\"/doc/1232445/\" id=\"a_12\">in that case</a> observed as follows:</span>\n</p>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_22\">     \u201c75. In order that the right guaranteed by clause (1) of\n     <a href=\"/doc/981147/\" id=\"a_13\">Article 32</a> be meaningful, and particularly because\n     such petitions seek the protection of fundamental\n     rights, it is imperative that in such proceedings the\n     petitioners are not denied the information necessary\n     for them to properly articulate the case and be heard,\n     especially where such information is in the possession\n     of the State. To deny access to such information,\n     without citing any constitutional principle or\n     enumerated grounds of constitutional prohibition,\n     would be to thwart the right granted by clause (1) of\n     <a href=\"/doc/981147/\" id=\"a_14\">Article 32.</a>\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_23\">     76. Further, inasmuch as, by history and tradition of\n     common law, judicial proceedings are substantively,\n     though not necessarily fully, adversarial, both\n     parties bear the responsibility of placing all the\n     relevant information, analyses, and facts before\n     this Court as completely as possible. In most\n     situations, it is the State which may have more\n     comprehensive information that is relevant to the\n     matters at hand in such proceedings...\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_24\">     77. It is necessary for us to note that the burden of\n     asserting, and proving, by relevant evidence a claim\n     in judicial proceedings would ordinarily be placed\n     upon the proponent of such a claim; however, the\n     burden of protection of fundamental rights is\n     primarily the duty of the State. Consequently,\n     unless constitutional grounds exist, the State\n     may not act in a manner that hinders this Court\n     from rendering complete justice in such\n     proceedings. Withholding of information from the\n     petitioners, or seeking to cast the relevant events and\n     facts in a light favourable to the State in the context\n     of the proceedings, even though ultimately\n     detrimental to the essential task of protecting\n     fundamental rights, would be destructive to the\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_37\">                                31</span>\n\f     guarantee in clause (1) of Article 32\u2026\n\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_25\">     78. In the task of upholding of fundamental\n     rights, the State cannot be an adversary. The\n     State has the duty, generally, to reveal all the\n     facts and information in its possession to the\n     Court, and also provide the same to the\n     petitioners. This is so, because the petitioners would\n     also then be enabled to bring to light facts and the\n     law that may be relevant for the Court in rendering\n     its decision. In proceedings such as those under\n     <a href=\"/doc/981147/\" id=\"a_15\">Article 32</a>, both the petitioner and the State, have to\n     necessarily be the eyes and ears of the Court.\n</blockquote><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_56\">     Blinding the petitioner would substantially detract\n     from the integrity of the process of judicial decision\u00ad\n     making in <a href=\"/doc/981147/\" id=\"a_16\">Article 32</a> proceedings, especially where\n     the issue is of upholding of fundamental rights.\u201d\n                                      (emphasis supplied)\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_57\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"82461587\" id=\"span_38\">47. This free flow of information from the Petitioners and the\n\nState, in a writ proceeding before the Court, is an important step\n\ntowards Governmental transparency and openness, which are\n\ncelebrated values under our Constitution, as recognized by this\n\nCourt recently in the <a href=\"/doc/82461587/\" id=\"a_17\">Anuradha Bhasin</a> (supra) judgment.</span>\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_58\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"1232445\" id=\"span_39\">48. Of course, there may be circumstances where the State has\n\na constitutionally defensible reason for denying access to certain\n\ninformation or divulging certain information as was recognized by\n\nthis Court in the <a href=\"/doc/1232445/\" id=\"a_18\">Ram Jethmalani</a> (supra) case, as extracted\n\nbelow:</span>\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_59\">\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_60\">     \u201c80.   Withholding     of   information    from    the\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_40\">                                 32</span>\n\f     petitioners by the State, thereby constraining their\n     freedom of speech and expression before this Court,\n     may be premised only on the exceptions carved\n     out, in clause (2) of <a href=\"/doc/1218090/\" id=\"a_19\">Article 19</a>, \u201cin the interests of\n     the sovereignty and integrity of India, the\n     security of the State, friendly relations with\n     foreign States, public order, decency or morality,\n     or in relation to contempt of court, defamation or\n     incitement to an offence\u201d or by law that\n     demarcate exceptions, provided that such a law\n     comports with the enumerated grounds in clause\n     (2) of <a href=\"/doc/1218090/\" id=\"a_20\">Article 19</a>, or that may be provided for\n     elsewhere in the Constitution.\u201d\n                                      (emphasis supplied)\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"RespArg\" id=\"p_61\">49. It is on the strength of the above exception carved out that\n\nthe Respondent\u00adUnion of India has justified its non\u00adsubmission\n\nof a detailed counter affidavit, viz., by citing security concerns. It\n\nis a settled position of law that in matters pertaining to national\n\nsecurity, the scope of judicial review is limited. However, this\n\ndoes not mean that the State gets a free pass every time the\n\nspectre of \u201cnational security\u201d is raised. National security cannot\n\nbe the bugbear that the judiciary shies away from, by virtue of its\n\nmere mentioning. Although this Court should be circumspect in\n\nencroaching upon the domain of national security, no omnibus\n\nprohibition can be called for against judicial review.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_62\">50. Of course, the Respondent\u00adUnion of India may decline to\n\nprovide information when constitutional considerations exist,\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_41\">                                  33</span>\n\fsuch as those pertaining to the security of the State, or when\n\nthere is a specific immunity under a specific statute. However, it\n\nis incumbent on the State to not only specifically plead such\n\nconstitutional concern or statutory immunity but they must also\n\nprove and justify the same in Court on affidavit. The Respondent\u00ad\n\nUnion of India must necessarily plead and prove the facts which\n\nindicate that the information sought must be kept secret as their\n\ndivulgence would affect national security concerns. They must\n\njustify the stand that they take before a Court. The mere\n\ninvocation of national security by the State does not render the\n\nCourt a mute spectator.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_63\">51. In the present matter, as we have indicated above, the\n\nPetitioners have placed on record certain material that prima\n\nfacie merits consideration by this Court. There has been no\n\nspecific denial of any of the facts averred by the Petitioners by\n\nthe Respondent\u00adUnion of India. There has only been an omnibus\n\nand   vague   denial   in   the   \u201climited   affidavit\u201d   filed   by   the\n\nRespondent\u00adUnion of India, which cannot be sufficient. In such\n\ncircumstances, we have no option but to accept the prima facie\n\ncase made out by the Petitioners to examine the allegations\n\nmade.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_64\">\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_42\">                                  34</span>\n</p><p data-structure=\"RespArg\" id=\"p_65\">\f52. Different forms of surveillance and data gathering by\n\nintelligence agencies to fight terrorism, crime and corruption in\n\nnational interest and/or for national security, are accepted\n\nnorms all over the world. The Petitioners do not contend that the\n\nState should not resort to surveillance/collection of data in\n\nmatters of national security. The complaint of the Petitioners is\n\nabout the misuse or likely misuse of spyware in violation of the\n\nright to privacy of citizens. The Respondent\u00adUnion of India also\n\ndoes   not   contend     that   its    agencies     can   resort    to\n\nsurveillance/collection of data relating to its citizens where\n\nnational security and national interest are not involved. The\n\napprehension of the Respondent\u00adUnion of India is that any\n\ninquiry in this behalf should not jeopardize national security and\n\nthe steps taken by it to protect national security. There is thus a\n\nbroad consensus that unauthorized surveillance/accessing of\n\nstored data from the phones and other devices of citizens for\n\nreasons   other   than   nation\u2019s     security    would   be   illegal,\n\nobjectionable and a matter of concern.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"RespArg\" id=\"p_66\">53. The only question that remains then is what the appropriate\n\nremedy in this case would be. Mr. Shyam Divan, learned senior\n\ncounsel appearing on behalf of the Petitioner in Writ Petition (C)\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_43\">                                35</span>\n\fNo. 849 of 2021 sought an interim order from this Court\n\ndirecting the Cabinet Secretary to put certain facts on an\n\naffidavit. On the other hand, most of the other senior counsel\n\nappearing on behalf of the other Writ Petitioners sought an\n\nindependent   investigation   or        inquiry   into   the   allegations\n\npertaining to the use of Pegasus software either by constituting a\n\nSpecial Investigation Team headed by a retired judge or by a\n\nJudges\u2019 Committee.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_67\">54. We are of the opinion that in the circumstances of the\n\npresent case, when the Respondent\u00adUnion of India has already\n\nbeen given multiple opportunities to file an affidavit on record,\n\nand looking to the conduct of the Respondent\u00adUnion of India in\n\nnot placing on record any facts through their reliance on the\n\n\u201cnational security\u201d defense, no useful purpose would be served\n\nby issuing directions of the nature sought by Mr. Shyam Divan,\n\napart from causing a further delay in proceedings.\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_68\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"65295726\" id=\"span_44\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"5616782\" id=\"span_45\"><span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"1232445\" id=\"span_46\">55. Instead, we are inclined to pass an order appointing an\n\nExpert Committee whose functioning will be overseen by a retired\n\nJudge of the Supreme Court. Such a course of action has been\n\nadopted by this Court in various other circumstances when the\n\nCourt found it fit in the facts and circumstances of the case to\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_47\">                                   36</span>\n\fprobe the truth or falsity of certain allegations, taking into\n\naccount the public importance and the alleged scope and nature\n\nof the large\u00adscale violation of the fundamental rights of the\n\ncitizens of the country [See <a href=\"/doc/1232445/\" id=\"a_21\">Ram Jethmalani</a> (supra); <a href=\"/doc/5616782/\" id=\"a_22\">Extra\u00ad\n\nJudicial Execution Victim Families Association v. Union of\n\nIndia</a>, (2013) 2 SCC 493; <a href=\"/doc/65295726/\" id=\"a_23\">G.S. Mani v. Union of India</a>, order\n\ndated 12.12.2019 in W.P. (Crl.) No. 348 of 2019].</span></span></span>\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_69\">56. The compelling circumstances that have weighed with us to\n\npass such an order are as follows:\n</p>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_26\">  i.    Right to privacy and freedom of speech are alleged to be\n\n        impacted, which needs to be examined.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_27\">  ii.   The entire citizenry is affected by such allegations due to\n\n        the potential chilling effect.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_28\"> iii.   No clear stand taken by the Respondent\u00adUnion of India\n\n        regarding actions taken by it.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_29\"> iv.    Seriousness     accorded   to    the   allegations   by   foreign\n\n        countries and involvement of foreign parties.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_30\">  v.    Possibility that some foreign authority, agency or private\n\n        entity is involved in placing citizens of this country under\n\n        surveillance.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_31\"> vi.    Allegations that the Union or State Governments are party\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_48\">                                    37</span>\n\f       to the rights\u2019 deprivations of the citizens.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_32\">vii.   Limitation under writ jurisdiction to delve into factual\n\n       aspects. For instance, even the question of usage of the\n\n       technology on citizens, which is the jurisdictional fact, is\n\n       disputed and requires further factual examination.\n</blockquote>\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_70\">57. It is for reason (vi) above that we decline the Respondent\u00ad\n\nUnion of India\u2019s plea to allow them to appoint an Expert\n\nCommittee for the purposes of investigating the allegations, as\n\nsuch a course of action would violate the settled judicial principle\n\nagainst bias, i.e., that \u2018justice must not only be done, but also be\n\nseen to be done\u2019.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_71\">58. At this juncture, it would be appropriate to state that in this\n\nworld of conflicts, it was an extremely uphill task to find and\n\nselect experts who are free from prejudices, are independent and\n\ncompetent. Rather than relying upon any Government agencies\n\nor any, we have constituted the Committee and shortlisted expert\n\nmembers     based    on    biodatas    and    information   collected\n\nindependently. Some of the candidates politely declined this\n\nassignment, while others had some conflict of interest. With our\n\nbest intentions and efforts, we have shortlisted and chosen the\n\nmost renowned experts available to be a part of the Committee.\n\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_49\">                                  38</span>\n\fAdditionally, we have also left it to the discretion of the learned\n\noverseeing judge to take assistance from any expert, if necessary,\n\nto ensure absolute transparency and efficiency, as directed in\n\nparagraph 62(3).\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_72\">59. With the above observations, we constitute a Technical\n\nCommittee comprising of three members, including those who\n\nare experts in cyber security, digital forensics, networks and\n\nhardware, whose functioning will be overseen by Justice R.V.\n\nRaveendran, former Judge, Supreme Court of India. The learned\n\noverseeing Judge will be assisted in this task by:\n\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_33\">      i.   Mr. Alok Joshi, former IPS officer (1976 batch) who\n\n           has immense and diverse investigative experience and\n\n           technical knowledge. He has worked as the Joint\n\n           Director,   Intelligence   Bureau,    the   Secretary(R),\n\n           Research and Analysis Wing and Chairman, National\n\n           Technical Research Organisation.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_34\">     ii. Dr. Oberoi is also a\n\n           part of the Advisory Board of Cyber Security Education\n\n           and Research Centre at Indraprastha Institute of\n\n           Information     Technology,      Delhi.   He   is     globally\n\n           recognized as a cyber security expert.\n</blockquote>\n\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_73\">60. The    three      members   Technical     Committee    [hereinafter\n\nreferred to as the \u201cCommittee\u201d] shall comprise of:\n\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_35\">      i.   Dr.   Naveen    Kumar        Chaudhary,   Professor    (Cyber\n\n           Security and Digital Forensics) and Dean, National\n\n           Forensic Sciences University, Gandhinagar, Gujarat.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_36\">           Dr. Chaudhary has over two decades of experience as\n\n           an academician, cyber security enabler and cyber\n\n           security expert. His areas of interest are malware\n\n           detection, critical infrastructural security, complex\n\n           binary analysis, AI and machine learning. He has\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_51\">                                   40</span>\n\f            many publications in reputed journals.\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_38\">    iii.
