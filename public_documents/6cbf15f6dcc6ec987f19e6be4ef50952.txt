Be that as it may, if the Vice-Chancellor\n invoked     the   special   powers     under <a href=\"/doc/156674088/\" id=\"a_6\">Section\n 13(2)</a> exercising the powers vested on Academic\n Council, the question that would arise for\n consideration is whether the Vice-Chancellor could\n exercise such powers, in the matter of\n promulgation of Ordinances which is a subordinate\n legislation. As rightly pointed out by the learned\n Senior Counsel Sri Arun Kumar K., subordinate\n legislation should not be manifestly arbitrary. <span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"1490250\" id=\"span_25\">In\n the case of Cellular Operators Association of\n India (supra), Their Lordships have noticed two\n judgments in the case of <a href=\"/doc/1490250/\" id=\"a_7\">Khoday Distilleries Ltd.\n Vs. State of Karnataka</a> (1996) 10 SCC 304 and\n Sharma Transport Vs. State of A.P., (2002) 2 SCC\n 188, where it was held that the tests of arbitrary\n action which apply to executive actions do not\n necessarily apply to delegated legislation. In order\n that delegated legislation can be struck down such\n legislation must be manifestly arbitrary; a law\n which could not be reasonably expected to\n emanate from an authority delegated with the law\n making power.
