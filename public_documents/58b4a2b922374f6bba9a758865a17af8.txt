After appreciating the deposition of the witnesses\n\n      and the relevant documents available on record,\n\n      the court found sufficient material to proceed\n\n      against    the     accused    persons   for   the   offence\n\n      punishable under <a href=\"/doc/1408202/\" id=\"a_4\">Section 500</a> IPC and accordingly\n\n      issued summons for appearance of the accused\n\n      persons in the court.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Issue\" id=\"p_3\">\n</p><p data-structure=\"Issue\" id=\"p_4\">3.    Aggrieved by the said order of summoning dated\n\n      20.12.2006, the present petition has been filed by\n\n      the petitioner Bank.         It is made clear that other\n\n      accused persons who happen to be the employees\n\n      of the bank are not a party to this petition and have\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_3\">Crl.M.C. Complainant had earlier filed a suit for damages\n\n      against the petitioner bank on 13.11.2002 alleging\n\n      that the bank had wrongly demanded payment of\n\n      credit card dues from him thereby causing him\n\n      mental harassment as well as the said demand\n\n      lowered his image and prestige in the eyes of\n\n      others including his family members. The present\n\n      complaint was filed on 4.2.2003. The civil suit filed\n\n      by the complainant (respondent herein) has been\n\n      decreed by the Civil Judge whereby a sum of\n\n      Rs.2,50,000/- with costs besides future interest @\n\n      8% per annum was awarded to the complainant.\n\n      The said amount admittedly has been paid by the\n\n      petitioner bank in full and final settlement of the\n\n      decretal amount.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Issue\" id=\"p_7\">\n</p><p data-structure=\"PetArg\" id=\"p_8\">5.     Mr.    Sanjay       Gupta,   learned    counsel   for   the\n\n      petitioner         has   submitted      that   perusal    of\n\n      letters/correspondences addressed by the bank to\n\n      the complainant would only indicate that demand\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_4\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                    Page 4 of 18</span>\n       was made bonafidely and the ingredients of <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_5\">Section\n\n      499</a> IPC which defines &quot;defamation&quot; are not made\n\n      out as per averments contained in the complaint.\n\n      He further argued that the letters were written by\n\n      the bank bonafidely.          The criminal intention i.e.\n\n      malice on the part of the petitioner bank to harm\n\n      the reputation of the complainant which is pre-\n\n      requisite of <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_6\">Section 499</a> IPC is missing in the\n\n      complaint.         Bank had no intention to harm the\n\n      reputation         of   the    complainant     when        it\n\n      corresponded with him with a view to get cleared\n\n      the due amount of Credit Card as per their own\n\n      records. He has further submitted that the conduct\n\n      of the petitioner bank falls in the 9th exception of\n\n      <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_7\">Section 499</a> IPC and, therefore, the Magistrate\n\n      without appreciating the facts and circumstances\n\n      of the case erroneously took cognizance of the\n\n      offence and summoned the petitioner for offence\n\n      punishable under <a href=\"/doc/1408202/\" id=\"a_8\">Section 500</a> IPC. He has prayed\n\n      that complaint, therefore, be quashed.\n\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"PetArg\" id=\"p_9\">6.     Mr. Sidharth Yadav, learned counsel for the\n\n      complainant         (respondent    No.1   herein)     while\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_5\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                    Page 5 of 18</span>\n       refuting the submission of the learned counsel for\n\n      the petitioner has argued that the correspondence\n\n      of the bank received by the petitioner, especially\n\n      the credit card statement and the telegram dated\n\n      17.9.2002 contain defamatory words and these\n\n      documents were seen by his family members and\n\n      also that Mr. Jishant Narang, accused No.4, also\n\n      talked to the wife of the complainant on telephone\n\n      and    intimated         her   that   the   account   of   the\n\n      complainant was cleared and there were no dues to\n\n      be claimed from him and that complainant should\n\n      ignore any letter which might be received from the\n\n      petitioner bank in future.              He has, therefore,\n\n      emphasized that defamation within the meaning of\n\n      <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_9\">Section 499</a> IPC is made out against the petitioner\n\n      bank     and       the   court    has   rightly   taken    the\n\n      cognizance of the offence and issued summons for\n\n      appearance against the bank and other accused\n\n      persons.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_10\">\n</p><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_11\">7.    For an offence of defamation as defined under\n\n      <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_10\">Section 499</a> IPC, three essential ingredients are\n\n      required to be fulfilled:-\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_12\"><span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_6\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                      Page 6 of 18</span>\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_1\">       (i)       Making     or     publishing     any    imputation\n\n                concerning any person;\n</blockquote>\n\n<blockquote id=\"blockquote_2\">      (ii)      Such imputation must have been made by\n\n                words either spoken or intended to be read\n\n                or by signs or by visible representations.</blockquote><pre id=\"pre_2\">\n\n\n      (iii)     The said imputation must have been made\n\n                with     the    intention   to   harm    or   with\n\n</pre><blockquote id=\"blockquote_3\">                knowledge or having reason to believe that\n\n                it will harm the reputation of the person\n\n                concerned.\n</blockquote>\n\n<p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_13\">8.     Thus, it is clear that intention to cause harm is the\n\n      most essential sine qua non for an offence under\n\n      <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_11\">Section 499</a> IPC.          An offence punishable under\n\n      <a href=\"/doc/1408202/\" id=\"a_12\">Section 500</a> IPC requires blameworthy mind and is\n\n      not a statutory offence requiring no mens rea.\n\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_14\">9.    9th Exception of the Section takes away the\n\n      imputation made in good faith by a person for\n\n      protection of his or other\u201fs interest or for public\n\n      good from the purview of defamation as defined in\n\n      the Section. This exception relates to private\n\n      communication which a person makes in good faith\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_7\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                     Page 7 of 18</span>\n       for   the   protection   of   his   own   interest.   This\n\n      exception covers not only such allegations of facts\n\n      as can be proved true but also expression of\n\n      opinions and personal inferences.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_15\">\n</p><p data-structure=\"Section\" id=\"p_16\">10.   9th exception has been incorporated to protect the\n\n      interests of the parties in their business transaction\n\n      which are generally done bonafidely and, therefore,\n\n      the rule of public good on which this principle is\n\n      based is, that honest transaction of business and\n\n      social intercourse would otherwise be deprived of\n\n      the protection which they should enjoy.\n\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_17\">11.   Whether any imputation made is with a motive or\n\n      malafide intention to lower the reputation or is\n\n      made in good faith is to be determined from the\n\n      facts and circumstances of the case. Undisputedly,\n\n      the requirement of good faith and public good,\n\n      both, are to be satisfied and the failure to prove\n\n      good faith would exclude the application of 9th\n\n      exception in favour of the accused even if the\n\n      requirement of public good is satisfied. The words\n\n      \u201egood faith\u201f as appearing in exception 9th not only\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_8\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                 Page 8 of 18</span>\n       require logical infallibility but also due care and\n\n      attention.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_18\">\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_19\">12.   The court has to consider as to how far erroneous\n\n      actions or statements are to be imputed for want of\n\n      due care and caution in a case in reference to the\n\n      general       circumstances,         the   capacity       and\n\n      intelligence of the person whose conduct is in\n\n      question. It is difficult to lay down any hard and\n\n      fast rule for deciding whether an accused acted in\n\n      good faith within the meaning of 9th exception, as it\n\n      is an issue to be considered on the facts and\n\n      circumstances of each case, nature of imputation\n\n      made, the circumstances under which it was made,\n\n      the status of the person who made it, and if there\n\n      was a malice in his mind when he made such\n\n      imputation, whether he made any inquiry before\n\n      any such imputation was made and if there were\n\n      reasons to accept his story, that he acted with due\n\n      care    and        attention   and   was   satisfied     that\n\n      imputation was true.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_20\">\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_21\">13.   <span class=\"citetext\" data-sentiment=\"Pos\" data-docid=\"859435\" id=\"span_9\">In &quot;<a href=\"/doc/859435/\" id=\"a_13\">Harbhajan Singh v. State of Punjab</a>, (1965)\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_10\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                      Page 9 of 18</span>\n       2 SCR 235&quot;, Exception 9 of <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_14\">Section 499</a> IPC has\n\n      been interpreted in para 20 and 21 as follows:-</span>\n\n\n</p><blockquote id=\"blockquote_4\">             &quot;20. Another       aspect    of     this\n             requirement      has    been     pithily\n             expressed by the Bombay High\n             Court in the case of Emperor v.\n</blockquote><blockquote id=\"blockquote_5\">             Abdool Wadood Ahmed.             &quot;Good\n             faith,&quot; it was observed &quot;requires not\n             indeed logical infallibility, but due\n             care and attention. But how far\n             erroneous actions or statements are\n             to be imputed to want of due care\n             and caution must, in each case, be\n             considered with reference to the\n             general circumstances and the\n             capacity and intelligence of the\n             person     whose     conduct    is    in\n             question&quot;.      &quot;it is only to be\n             expected&quot;, says the judgment, &quot;that\n             the honest conclusions of a calm and\n             philosophical mind may differ very\n             largely from the honest conclusions\n             of a person excited by sectarian zeal\n             and untrained to habits of precise\n             reasoning.     At the same time, it\n             must be borne in mind that good\n             faith in the formation or expression\n             of an opinion, can afford no\n             protection to an imputation which\n             does not purport to be based on that\n             which is the legitimate subject of\n             public comment.&quot;\n</blockquote>\n<p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_22\">             21. Thus, it would be clear that in\n             deciding whether an accused person\n             acted in good faith under the Ninth\n             Exception, it is not possible to lay\n             down any rigid rule or test. It would\n             be a question to be considered on\n             the facts and circumstances of each\n             case - what is the nature of the\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_11\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                  Page 10 of 18</span>\n              imputation     made;    under    what\n             circumstances did it come to be\n             made; what is the status of the\n             person who makes the imputation;\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_23\">             was there any malice in his mind\n             when he made the said imputation;\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_24\">             did he make any enquiry before he\n             made it; are there reasons to accept\n             his story that he acted with due care\n             and attention and was satisfied that\n             the imputation was true? These and\n             other considerations would be\n             relevant in deciding the plea of good\n             faith made by an accused person\n             who claims the benefit of the Ninth\n             Exception...................&quot;\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_25\">\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_26\">14.   Telegram       dated   17.9.2002    received     by    the\n\n      complainant at his house and allegedly read by his\n\n      family members i.e. his wife reads as below:-\n\n\n</p><pre id=\"pre_3\">                   &quot;CARD     NO.5404     6112   0055\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_12\">                   5006</span>\n\n                   TOTAL              AMOUNT\n                   OUTSTANDING             IS\n                   RS.362373.01 AAA DESPITE\n                   AMPTEEN       EFFORTS  TO\n                   CONTACT YOU AT YOUR\n                   OFFICE     AS    WELL  AS\n                   RESIDENCE NUMBER AAA WE\n                   HAVE NOT HEARD FROM YOU\n                   SO FAR AAA YOUR CARD\n                   ACCOUNT IS IN A SERIOUS\n                   STAGE OR DELIQUENCY AND\n                   ANY FUTHER DELAY ON\n                   YOUR PART TO ADDRESS THE\n                   MATTER MAY PROOF COSTLY\n                   IN TERMS OF MONEY AS\n                   WELL AS LITIGATION/BOTH\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_13\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                 Page 11 of 18</span>\n                    CIVIL  AND    CRIMINAL/IN\n                   YOUR NAME CALL BANK\n                   RIGHT AWAY AT 3705254.&quot;\n\n\n\n</pre><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_27\">15.   According to the complainant the imputation on his\n\n      character in this telegram were &quot; and any further\n\n      delay on your part to address the matter may prove\n\n      costly in terms of money as well as litigation/both\n\n      civil and criminal/in your name&quot;. This telegram in\n\n      no manner can be considered as defamatory in\n\n      nature. This telegram only expressed the concern\n\n      of the bank/petitioner to get the dues of the credit\n\n      card cleared well in time and in case there was\n\n      default, it would invite criminal as well as civil\n\n      liability.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_28\">\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_29\">16.   Mens Rea; a mandatory pre-requisite of an offence\n\n      of defamation      is   clearly    missing in   the    said\n\n      communication.          This      communication       made\n\n      bonafidely by the petitioner bank upon the subject\n\n      matter contained therein, in which the petitioner\n\n      had an interest or it had the duty to correspond\n\n      with the complainant asking him to clear the dues\n\n      under the circumstances would be privileged and\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_14\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                  Page 12 of 18</span>\n       would attract exception 9th. Petitioner bank had no\n\n      reason to lower the dignity and character of the\n\n      complainant in the eyes of anyone. The bank was\n\n      not reckless in sending this telegram to the\n\n      complainant. The complainant upon responding to\n\n      the correspondence though denied his liability to\n\n      pay the amount having no concern with the card in\n\n      question as he never held the said card in his\n\n      name. Action of the bank was in good faith as also\n\n      in    public       good    as   the   entire    process   of\n\n      correspondence with the complainant was with a\n\n      view to protect the public money safely invested\n\n      with the bank and found due from the complainant\n\n      (as per the bank\u201fs record) was repaid.\n\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_30\">17.   Besides, requirement of publishing any imputation\n\n      concerning the complainant is also missing in this\n\n      case.    This telegram was sent to the complainant\n\n      only. The alleged information by accused No.4 to\n\n      the wife of the complainant cannot in any manner\n\n      be    considered      as    defamatory.   The    intimation\n\n      communicated to the wife of the complainant was\n\n      that there were no dues left to be claimed in the\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_15\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                   Page 13 of 18</span>\n       account of the complainant and in case any\n\n      communication was received from the bank in\n\n      future, the same should be ignored.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_31\">\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_32\">18.   This information in no manner lowered the dignity\n\n      of the complainant in the eyes of his wife.         This\n\n      intimation was bonafidely made with a view to save\n\n      the complainant from future harassment at the\n\n      hands of the petitioner and other accused persons.\n\n      The wife of the complainant on receipt of the\n\n      information on telephone from accused No.4 of her\n\n      own motion made inquiries from friends of the\n\n      complainant about the alleged account without any\n\n      realm or reason and, therefore, such inquiries\n\n      made by her from the friends of the complainant do\n\n      not invite the provisions of <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_15\">Section 499</a>/<a href=\"/doc/1408202/\" id=\"a_16\">500</a> IPC.\n\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"CDiscource\" id=\"p_33\">19.   The Civil Court in a suit for mandatory injunction\n\n      and     for   damages   decreed   the   suit   of    the\n\n      complainant awarding damages to him against the\n\n      bank.    The observations of the Civil Court in the\n\n      said suit that the persistent acts of the bank\n\n      without any ground was defamatory in nature and\n\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_16\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                               Page 14 of 18</span>\n       harmed the reputation of the complainant might be\n\n      relevant, however, they are not conclusive and\n\n      binding on the Magistrate to be followed and\n\n      accepted. The reason being the Civil Court has to\n\n      appreciate the evidence of the plaintiff in a suit for\n\n      damages based on defamation with a different\n\n      yardstick and is not required to assess the evidence\n\n      with a view to find out if any criminality was\n\n      involved.     In other words, the Civil Court is not\n\n      concerned whether such acts of defamation were\n\n      malafidely done with an intention to lower and\n\n      harm the reputation of the plaintiff in the eyes of\n\n      his family members and others.      For inviting the\n\n      provisions contained in <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_17\">Section 499</a>/<a href=\"/doc/1408202/\" id=\"a_18\">500</a> IPC which\n\n      are penal in nature, a Magistrate has to consider if\n\n      the requirement of mens rea which is a mandate\n\n      for a criminal defamation punishable under <a href=\"/doc/1408202/\" id=\"a_19\">Section\n\n      500</a> IPC was fulfilled.     If mens rea or criminal\n\n      intention is lacking or is missing in the act of the\n\n      accused, he cannot be held guilty for an offence of\n\n      defamation within the meaning of <a href=\"/doc/1041742/\" id=\"a_20\">Section 499</a> IPC.\n\n      In this case the image or reputation of the\n\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_17\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                             Page 15 of 18</span>\n       complainant was not tarnished in any manner by\n\n      the petitioner bank.       None of the correspondence\n\n      were ever published or sent to any other person\n\n      other than the complainant himself. Besides, none\n\n      of these correspondence indicate that the bank\n\n      used such language in the letters sent to the\n\n      complaint which could be termed as defamatory,\n\n      especially the telegram dated 17.9.2002 or the\n\n      telephone call received by his wife. Therefore,\n\n      prima facie, the complaint did not disclose any\n\n      offence     of     defamation   made   out   against   the\n\n      petitioner bank.\n</p>\n<p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_34\">\n</p><p data-structure=\"Precedent\" id=\"p_35\">20.   Undisputedly, the petitioner is a bank incorporated\n\n      in England with limited liability by Royal Charter,\n\n      1853 and, therefore, is a corporation/company. A\n\n      company cannot be in any case held to have\n\n      committed an offence under <a href=\"/doc/1408202/\" id=\"a_21\">Section 500</a> IPC\n\n      because, most essential ingredient of the said\n\n      offence i.e. \u201emens rea\u201f would be missing as a\n\n      company is a juristic entity or an artificial person,\n\n      whereas a Director is not a company. The company\n\n      may be made liable for offences, however, if there\n\n<span class=\"hidden_text\" id=\"span_18\">Crl.M.C. No. 3828/2007                                 Page 16 of 18</span>\n       is anything in the definition or context of a\n\n      particular Section of a particular statute which\n\n      would prevent the application of the said section to\n\n      a limited company, the limited company cannot be\n\n      proceeded against. There are number of provisions\n\n      of law in which it would be physically impossible by\n\n      a limited company to commit the offence. A limited\n\n      company, therefore, cannot generally be tried for\n\n      offences where mens rea is essential. Similarly, a\n\n      company        cannot    face     the   punishment      of\n\n      imprisonment for obvious reasons that company\n\n      cannot be sent to prison by way of a sentence.\n\n\n</p><p data-structure=\"Conclusion\" id=\"p_36\">21.
