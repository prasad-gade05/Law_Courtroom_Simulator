**Prosecution’s Rebuttal to the Defence’s Claim of a Hacked Account**

Your Honor, the defence has sought to portray this matter as a simple
technical mishap – that Mr. Rohan Malhotra’s social‑media accounts were
“hacked” and that, consequently, he bears no liability for the defamatory and
harassing posts. While the prosecution respects the seriousness of
cyber‑security breaches, the burden of proof rests squarely on the accused to
establish such a defence beyond reasonable doubt. The record, however,
contains nothing that meets that standard.

**1. The statutory elements of defamation are satisfied.**   Section 499 of
the Indian Penal Code defines defamation as the “publication… of any
imputation concerning any person… intending to harm, or knowing or having
reason to believe that such imputation will harm the reputation of such
person” [IPC‑1]. The posts in question – captured in the screenshots provided
by Ms. Meera Sharma – alleged that she was involved in bribery, biased
reporting and professional misconduct. These imputations are expressly the
kind of statements that lower a person’s moral and intellectual character in
the eyes of society [OTH‑4]. The impact statement filed by Ms. Sharma details
loss of professional standing, anxiety and reputational damage, confirming the
“harm” contemplated by the statute [EVI‑1].

**2. Intent or knowledge can be inferred from the circumstances.**   The
prosecution does not need a confession of intent; it must show that the
accused either intended the harm or “had reason to believe” the imputation
would be harmful. The following facts establish that Rohan Malhotra possessed
such knowledge:

* The defamatory posts were made from his **verified** Twitter and Instagram
accounts – the very accounts that bear his name, followers and a history of
professional communication [EVI‑2]. * The timing of the posts coincides with a
known business dispute between Mr. Malhotra and Ms. Sharma, providing a clear
motive to tarnish her reputation [Case File summary]. * A **witness**,
Mr. Ravi Verma, testified that Mr. Malhotra had expressed concerns about the
security of his accounts **prior** to the incident, indicating he was aware of
potential misuse but nevertheless continued to operate the same
accounts [EVI‑1]. * The digital‑forensics report shows **suspicious login
activity from an unknown IP address** at the exact times the posts were
published [EVI‑1]. While this suggests a possible breach, the report does
**not** demonstrate that the IP address was unrelated to Mr. Malhotra. In
fact, it shows that his devices were being accessed, which is consistent with
an authorized user (the account holder) operating the account.

Under established jurisprudence, the existence of a plausible alternative
explanation (e.g., hacking) does not exonerate a defendant unless it is proven
on a balance of probabilities [no specific case is cited in the supplied
material, but the principle is implicit in the statutory burden].

**3. The alleged “hacking” defence fails under the Information Technology
Act.**   Section 66A of the IT Act penalises the sending of “offensive
messages … which cause annoyance or insult” [OTH‑1]. Even if the posts were
the product of an unauthorized intrusion, the **act** of publishing offensive
material through a communication service remains an offence. The law does not
provide a safe harbour for a person who fails to adequately secure his
accounts. The prosecution therefore contends that Mr. Malhotra, as the
**account owner**, is legally responsible for the content transmitted from his
credentials, absent a credible exculpatory explanation [EVI‑1].

**4. The defence’s arguments regarding style, character and prompt reporting
are irrelevant to liability.**   * **Writing style:** Defamation is a
content‑based offence; the manner of expression is immaterial. The law focuses
on the truth or falsity of the imputation, not on stylistic similarity. *
**Good character:** Prior good conduct does not negate the commission of a
specific offence. Section 499 is a strict liability provision concerning the
act of publication with knowledge of probable harm. * **Immediate reporting:**
Mr. Malhotra’s report to the cyber‑crime division occurred **after** the posts
had already been made and after the victim filed a complaint. This does not
erase the damage already inflicted nor does it establish lack of intent.

**5. The prosecution’s evidentiary package is complete and unambiguous.**   *
**Screenshots** of the defamatory posts [EVI‑1]   * **Digital‑forensics log**
showing the exact timestamps of the uploads from his accounts [EVI‑1]   *
**Witness testimony** establishing prior concern over account security [EVI‑1]
* **Impact statement** confirming reputational harm [EVI‑1]

Taken together, these pieces satisfy the legal requisites of Section 499 and,
concurrently, of Section 66A of the IT Act. The defence has offered no
forensic expert report that conclusively proves an external intrusion, nor any
independent audit of the alleged foreign IP address. The onus remains with the
accused, and that burden has not been met.

**Conclusion**   For the reasons articulated above, the prosecution submits
that the evidence establishes beyond reasonable doubt that Rohan Malhotra
**intentionally** or **with knowledge** that his posts would harm Ms. Sharma’s
reputation, and that he is liable under both the IPC and the IT Act. We
therefore respectfully request that the Court reject the hack‑defence and
proceed to convict on the charges of defamation (Section 499) and cyber
harassment (Section 66A).

**Potential hallucinations / factual / logical errors in the defense’s latest
argument**

1. **Mis‑statement of the law – Sec. 66A**      *The defense asserts that
Sec. 66A “penalises the transmission of offensive material … irrespective of
the sender’s intent” and imposes strict liability on the account holder.*
This is factually wrong. The Supreme Court, in *Shreya Singhal v. Union of
India* (2015), declared Sec. 66A unconstitutional for being vague and
violative of freedom of speech. It therefore **cannot** be the basis of a
criminal charge today. Citing it as a current provision is a legal error.

2. **Over‑reliance on “device usage” without expert corroboration**      The
defense claims the forensic report “confirms his devices were used at the
exact moments the defamatory posts were uploaded.” The prosecution’s summary
only notes “suspicious login activity from an unknown IP address.” Unless an
independent cybersecurity expert can tie the IP to Rohan’s device (e.g., MAC
address, device fingerprint), the assertion that the device *itself* was used
is unsubstantiated in the record presented so far. This is a logical gap.

3. **Incorrect characterization of Sec. 499 as “strict liability”**      While
Sec. 499 does not require malice in the same way as some offences, it does
require **knowledge** or **reason to believe** that the imputation will harm.
The defense’s statement that the provision is “strict liability … regardless
of how the account was accessed” downplays the statutory mental element. The
correct argument would centre on a *reasonable doubt* that Rohan had the
requisite knowledge, not that the provision is strict liability.

4. **Citation placeholders without supporting material**      The defense
repeatedly cites “[EVI‑1][EVI‑2]”. In the supplied evidence list, these
markers refer to screenshots, logs, and impact statements, but the defense
does not explain *how* those items support the claim of a hack. The argument
therefore leans on citations that do not directly substantiate the hack
defence.

**Constructive feedback for the defense**

- **Remove or replace the reliance on Sec. 66A.** If you wish to argue
liability under the IT Act, consider citing **Sec. 66** (punishment for
computer‑related offences) for the alleged intrusion, or the **newer
provisions on cyber harassment** (e.g., Sec. 354D IPC in conjunction with the
IT Act for “harassment by electronic means”). Explain why those sections do
*not* attach liability to Rohan for the content of the posts.

- **Strengthen the technical defence with expert testimony.** Present a
qualified cybersecurity expert who can:   1. Show that the foreign IP address
is *not* linked to any device owned or regularly used by Rohan.   2.
Demonstrate that the login timestamps correspond to a session initiated from a
location impossible for Rohan to be in (e.g., different time zone, impossible
travel time).   3. Explain why the “device usage” inference is unreliable
without such linkage.

- **Address the mental element of Sec. 499 directly.** Argue that the
prosecution has not proved *beyond reasonable doubt* that Rohan *knew* or *had
reason to believe* the statements would harm Meera Sharma. Emphasise:   * Lack
of prior pattern of defamatory posting.   * Distinct writing style that

IP 203.0.113.45 at 14:23 IST”) and tie it directly to your factual contention.

Your Honor, the defense respectfully submits the following rebuttals in light
of the prosecutor’s recent clarifications.

First, we acknowledge that Section 66A of the Information Technology Act is no
longer enforceable, having been declared unconstitutional in
*Shreya Singhal v. Union of India* (2015). Consequently, we will not rely on
that provision and instead focus solely on the defamation allegation under
Section 499 of the Indian Penal Code. The statutory definition of defamation
requires a “publication… by words… or signs… with intention to harm, or
knowing or having reason to believe that such imputation will harm” the
reputation of the person concerned [OTH‑4][EVI‑1].

Second, the prosecution’s case hinges on the premise that the posts originated
from the accused’s account. While the screenshots undeniably show the
statements, the forensic log records a “suspicious login activity from an
unknown IP address” at the exact moments the posts were uploaded [EVI‑1]. We
have engaged a certified cybersecurity expert who will testify that: (a) the
foreign IP is geolocated in a jurisdiction to which Mr. Malhotra has no
physical or virtual connection; (b) the timestamps correspond to a time‑zone
discrepancy that makes it impossible for him to have been the originator; and
(c) the login was made via a VPN service not used by Mr. Malhotra, thereby
severing any causal link between the accused and the defamatory content. This
expert testimony directly counters the prosecution’s suggestion that the IP
“could belong to the accused” and creates a reasonable doubt as to authorship.

Third, even assuming the posts were made from the accused’s account, the
prosecution has not satisfied the mental element required under Section 499.
The law obliges the Crown to prove that the accused either intended the harm
or had reason to believe the imputation would damage Meera Sharma’s
reputation. The record contains no evidence of such knowledge or belief:  (i)
Mr. Malhotra’s own contemporaneous complaint to the cyber‑crime division
demonstrates his lack of intent to defame [EVI‑1]; (ii) the witness, Ravi
Verma, attested that Mr. Malhotra had expressed prior concerns about the
security of his accounts, indicating his apprehension rather than a desire to
publish harmful content [EVI‑1]; (iii) linguistic analysis of the posts
reveals a style and tone markedly different from Mr. Malhotra’s usual
communications, undermining any inference of personal authorship. Absent proof
of either intent or a “reason to believe” the statements would be defamatory,
the statutory element remains unfulfilled.

Finally, the alleged business dispute cited by the prosecution is at best an
uncorroborated assertion. No documentary evidence—such as correspondence,
contracts, or prior complaints—has been produced to demonstrate a motive
linking the dispute to the defamatory imputation. The prosecution’s reliance
on motive alone cannot substitute for the required proof of knowledge or
belief under Section 499. Thus, the prosecution has not discharged its burden
of establishing the offence beyond reasonable doubt.

In sum, the defense submits that (1) the inviable Section 66A is abandoned;
(2) expert forensic testimony will disassociate the foreign IP from the
accused; (3) the mental element of Section 499 remains unproven; and (4) the
purported motive lacks evidentiary foundation. Accordingly, we urge the Court
to find that the prosecution has failed to meet its evidentiary burden and to
acquit Mr. Rohan Malhotra of the defamation charge.
