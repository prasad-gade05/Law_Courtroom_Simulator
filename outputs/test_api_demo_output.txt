permitted to inquire about the known vulnerabilities of SMS 2FA and prevailing

hypothetical or cutting-edge security measures not reasonably expected of the
average user. The inquiry must remain grounded in whether Mr. Malhotra acted
as a reasonably prudent user given the known risks at the time.

Mr. Malhotra was legally negligent, as this is a legal determination that
falls within the sole purview of the Court and jury.

technical aspects and known vulnerabilities of SMS 2FA, but must refrain from
asking Dr. Sharma to make definitive statements about legal negligence or to
offer opinions on whether Mr. Malhotra's actions constitute a breach of legal

mobile carrier's system to reroute the victim's phone number to a SIM card
controlled by the attacker. Once the number is rerouted, the attacker can then
receive SMS-based 2FA codes sent by the social media platform. Therefore, the
*social media login logs themselves* would likely only show a *successful
authentication* occurring after the SIM swap, potentially from an IP address
associated with the attacker's location or VPN, and importantly, with the
correct 2FA code received. The logs would not inherently contain specific
forensic markers *of the SIM swap itself*, as that event occurs at the mobile
carrier level, not on the social media platform's servers. Detecting a SIM
swap would require correlation with mobile carrier data or specific network
traffic analysis, which is typically beyond the scope of social media platform
logs alone.

2.  **Known Risks of SMS 2FA (Circa Dec 2023):** By December 2023, it was
widely known within cybersecurity circles that SMS 2FA was vulnerable. The
primary risks included:     *   **SIM Swapping:** As discussed, attackers
could trick mobile carriers into transferring the victim's phone number to a
SIM card they control.     *   **Phishing:** Attackers could trick users into
revealing both their password and the SMS code via fake login pages or
messages.     *   **Interception:** While less common for SMS, messages could
potentially be intercepted over networks.     *   **Replay Attacks:** In some
limited scenarios, intercepted codes could theoretically be reused.     The
general awareness was that while SMS 2FA offered a significant improvement
over single-factor authentication, it was not foolproof and was considered
less secure than application-based authenticators or hardware tokens due to
these vulnerabilities. Many security advisories and tech publications had
highlighted these risks for several years leading up to 2023.

3.  **Technical Process of SIM-Swapping with Compromised Credentials:** An
attacker would typically first aim to obtain the victim's login credentials
(username and password) through methods like phishing, purchasing them from
the dark web after a data breach on another service, or malware. Once they
have the password, they would initiate a SIM-swapping attack with the victim's
mobile carrier, often using social engineering tactics or exploiting carrier
vulnerabilities to gain control of the victim's phone number. After
successfully porting the number, the attacker would go to the social media
platform, enter the victim's password, and trigger the 2FA code. Because the
SMS code would be sent to the attacker's controlled SIM card, they could then
enter the code to complete the two-factor authentication and gain full access
to the account. This process bypasses the need for brute-force attacks on the
social media platform itself and relies on compromising the credentials and
the phone number separately.
